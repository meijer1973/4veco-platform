# DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS

This section outlines the philosophy and decisions behind the course structure. Read this before the detailed blueprint — it explains why things are where they are.

## 1. Exercise-first design

Every theory paragraph begins with a **target exercise**: the exercise a student should be able to complete by the end of that lesson (1 hour classroom + ~30 minutes homework). This is deliberate. Lesson goals written in abstract language ("the student can analyse…") lose precision fast. A concrete exercise makes it unambiguous what the student must actually *do*: read a graph, perform a calculation, draw a diagram, formulate an argument. It also immediately exposes whether a paragraph is too heavy or too light for one lesson.

## 2. Lean paragraphs, rich consolidation

Theory paragraphs are kept lean and focused on one core skill or concept per lesson. They deliberately avoid heavy use of extended text sources, data tables, and multi-source exam contexts. This is not because those skills are unimportant — they are essential — but because they belong in a different phase of learning.

**Consolidation paragraphs** (one per chapter, no new theory) are where students encounter exam-style packaging: longer texts, tables, graphs as source material, combined questions that require integrating multiple skills. This separation ensures that students first master the raw skill cleanly, then practice applying it under realistic exam conditions with more complex framing. Consolidation paragraphs should include contexts drawn from news articles, CBS data, EU policy documents, and other authentic sources.

## 3. Smooth difficulty distribution

A key design constraint was that **no single paragraph should require more than one lesson in practice**. Teaching experience shows that a lesson that unexpectedly takes two sessions disrupts the entire planning cascade. Every paragraph was assessed for realistic classroom time. Where content was too heavy for one lesson, it was split across paragraphs or redistributed across chapters. This is why, for example, cost structures are split across two paragraphs (totals/averages, then marginals), and monopoly profit maximisation is preceded by a separate "understanding MO at monopoly" lesson.

Book 4 is deliberately lighter than Books 2–3 to accommodate end-of-year fatigue and to serve as a buffer if earlier books run over schedule.

## 4. Paragraph and chapter structure

Each book has 5 chapters: 4 theory chapters and 1 test preparation chapter. Each theory chapter has 3 theory paragraphs and 1 consolidation paragraph (4 paragraphs total). Exception: Book 1 Chapter 4 has 4 theory paragraphs + consolidation (5 total), because marginal analysis needed to be split for didactic reasons.

Total lesson count: ~49 theory lessons + 16 consolidation + 4 test prep chapters ≈ 73–80 lessons. At 2 lessons per week across ~36 school weeks, this fits.

## 5. Building-block progression

The course follows a strict prerequisite chain. Each paragraph builds on earlier ones, and the blueprint explicitly avoids introducing concepts before their foundations are in place. Key progression:

- **Percentages and graphs** (Book 1 Ch1) → **elasticity calculations** (Book 2 Ch1)
- **Cost structures** (Book 1 Ch3§2) → **marginal costs** (Book 1 Ch4§3) → **MO=MK rule** (Book 1 Ch4§4) → **profit maximisation at volkomen concurrentie** (Book 3 Ch1§2) → **profit maximisation at monopoly** (Book 3 Ch2§3)
- **Surplus analysis** (Book 2 Ch2) → **welfare effects of taxes** (Book 2 Ch3) → **welfare effects of monopoly** (Book 3 Ch3§1) → **welfare effects of minimum wage on labor market** (Book 4 Ch2§2) → **welfare effects of tariffs** (Book 4 Ch3§3)

These chains mean that weakness in early skills cascades. The consolidation paragraphs serve as checkpoints to catch gaps before they become problems.

## 6. Differentiation arrives in economics before mathematics

Students encounter differentiation of simple quadratic functions for the first time in this economics course, typically before their mathematics course covers it. The blueprint handles this by introducing differentiation as a practical recipe ("power rule: bring down the exponent, reduce by one") applied to economic functions, not as a mathematical concept. The progression is:

1. Marginals from tables and subtraction (Book 1 Ch4§3)
2. Differentiating TK to find MK (Book 3 Ch1§2) — first use of the rule
3. Differentiating TO to find MO at monopoly (Book 3 Ch2§2–§3) — full application

We only need to differentiate quadratic equations. Coordinate with mathematics colleagues when Book 3 Chapter 2 is taught.

## 7. Context and motivation

While theory paragraphs are lean, they are not dry. Each target exercise uses a recognisable context (bakeries, cinemas, restaurants, the NS, solar panels). The purpose is dual: maintaining student motivation and beginning to build the transfer skill that exams require. However, the heavy contextual work — interpreting complex sources, combining multiple concepts in an unfamiliar setting — is concentrated in consolidation paragraphs and test preparation chapters.

## 8. Test design

Each book contains two test moments. The **smaller test (45 minutes)** comes mid-book, after chapters 1 and 2, to check whether students are on track and to catch mistakes early — before the material builds further. The **larger test (120 minutes)** comes at the end of the book, covering all four theory chapters, and is the main summative assessment. This rhythm means students get feedback halfway through each book, giving both teacher and students time to correct course before the high-stakes test. The consolidation paragraphs and test prep chapters explicitly prepare students for both formats.

# BOOK 1 — Foundations & Demand/Supply

## Chapter 1: Economic Thinking & Math Foundations

### §1 — Scarcity, opportunity cost, economic thinking ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> A farmer has 10 hectares of land. He can grow wheat (profit €500/ha) or corn (profit €350/ha). He decides to grow wheat on all 10 hectares.
> (a) What is his revenue from wheat?
> (b) What are his opportunity costs?
> (c) A neighbour uses 10 hectares for both: 6 ha wheat and 4 ha corn. Calculate her total profit. Did she make a better choice? Explain using the concept of scarcity.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define scarcity as the core economic problem
- Explain that every choice has an opportunity cost
- Calculate opportunity costs in simple scenarios
- Recognise trade-offs in everyday decisions

**Difficulty notes:** Intuitive concepts, concrete examples. Students engage well. No calculation barriers. One lesson is comfortable with time for class discussion.

---

### §2 — Percentages, percentage changes, index numbers 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> (a) The price of a bicycle rises from €800 to €920. Calculate the percentage change.
> (b) In 2023, a standard shopping basket costs €150. In 2025 the same basket costs €162. The base year is 2023 (index = 100). Calculate the price index for 2025.
> (c) In 2026 the index rises to 112. Calculate the price increase from 2025 to 2026 in percent.
> (d) A student claims: "The index went from 108 to 112, so inflation is 4%." Explain why this is wrong and calculate the correct figure.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate percentage changes using (new − old) / old × 100
- Calculate and interpret index numbers
- Distinguish between index point change and percentage change
- Apply these skills to economic data (prices, wages, production)

**Difficulty notes:** Students are weaker at percentage calculations than teachers expect. The index-point vs percentage trap (exercise d) is a classic exam mistake. This paragraph lays the foundation for ALL later calculations (elasticities, inflation, real/nominal). Worth investing time. One lesson is tight — assign exercise (d) type problems as homework.

---

### §3 — Reading and drawing graphs, interpreting tables ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> The table below shows the number of ice creams sold at different prices:
>
> | Price (€) | Quantity |
> |-----------|----------|
> | 1.00      | 500      |
> | 1.50      | 400      |
> | 2.00      | 300      |
> | 2.50      | 200      |
> | 3.00      | 100      |
>
> (a) Draw this as a graph with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis.
> (b) Read from your graph: how many ice creams are sold at €1.75?
> (c) A newspaper writes: "Ice cream sales dropped by 50%." Looking at the table, between which two prices could this have happened? Explain.

**Lesson goals:**
- Read values from graphs and tables
- Draw a graph from tabular data with correct axes
- Interpolate between data points
- Critically interpret claims using data

**Difficulty notes:** Skill-based, hands-on. Students enjoy the concreteness. The main pitfall is putting axes wrong (price on horizontal instead of vertical). One lesson comfortable.

---

### §4 — Consolidation ⬜

Extra practice combining all three skills: percentage calculations with graph reading, interpreting index numbers from a table, drawing graphs from data. Introduce first exam-style elements: a short text with a data table, followed by questions that require both reading and calculation. No new theory.

## Chapter 2: Demand

### §1 — Individual demand, betalingsbereidheid, demand curve ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> Lisa loves pizza. She is willing to pay €8 for her first pizza, €6 for the second, €4 for the third, and €1.50 for the fourth.
> (a) Draw Lisa's individual demand curve (step function) with price on the vertical axis.
> (b) The market price is €5. How many pizzas does Lisa buy? Explain using the concept of betalingsbereidheid.
> (c) The price drops to €3. How many does she buy now?
> (d) Explain in your own words why the demand curve slopes downward.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define betalingsbereidheid (willingness to pay)
- Explain the relationship between price and quantity demanded
- Draw an individual demand curve from willingness-to-pay data
- Explain the law of demand

**Difficulty notes:** Very intuitive. Pizza/real-life examples work well. Step functions are visual and concrete. One lesson comfortable — use the extra time for a good class discussion about what determines betalingsbereidheid.

---

### §2 — Demand factors, shifts vs movements along the curve 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The market for butter is in equilibrium at P = €2 and Q = 1000.
> (a) The price of butter rises to €2.50. Show in a graph what happens to the quantity demanded. Is this a shift of the demand curve or a movement along it?
> (b) A health report claims that margarine is unhealthy. Show in a graph what happens to the demand for butter. Is this a shift or a movement?
> (c) Average income in the country rises by 5%. Butter is a normal good. Show the effect on the demand curve.
> (d) Name four factors that cause the demand curve to shift and give an example for each.

**Lesson goals:**
- Distinguish between a movement along the demand curve (caused by own price change) and a shift of the demand curve (caused by other factors)
- Name and explain demand factors: income, preferences, price of substitutes/complements, expectations
- Show shifts graphically (left/right)
- Classify goods as substitutes or complements based on context

**Difficulty notes:** The shift vs movement distinction is the single most common error in all of microeconomics. Students confuse these for months. The lesson must hammer this with at least 4–5 examples. One lesson is tight but essential — this concept returns in every subsequent chapter.

---

### §3 — From individual to collective demand 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> There are only two consumers on the market for lemonade: Anna and Ben.
>
> | Price (€) | Anna's Qd | Ben's Qd |
> |-----------|-----------|----------|
> | 1         | 5         | 8        |
> | 2         | 3         | 5        |
> | 3         | 1         | 2        |
> | 4         | 0         | 1        |
>
> (a) Calculate the collective demand at each price.
> (b) Draw the individual demand curves and the collective demand curve in the same graph.
> (c) Anna's demand function is Qd = -2P + 7, Ben's is Qd = -3P + 11. Derive the collective demand function algebraically.
> (d) At which price does Anna leave the market entirely? What happens to the collective demand curve at that point?

**Lesson goals:**
- Explain that collective demand is the horizontal sum of individual demand curves
- Calculate collective demand from individual data (table method)
- Derive collective demand algebraically (adding Q's at equal P)
- Recognise the kink in the collective curve when one consumer drops out

**Difficulty notes:** The horizontal addition (adding Q's, not P's) is counter-intuitive for some students. The algebraic derivation (exercise c) requires comfort with linear equations. One lesson works if algebra skills from Ch1 are solid. The kink (exercise d) can be mentioned but is not essential.

---

### §4 — Consolidation ⬜

Practice exercises combining demand curves, shifts, and aggregation. Introduce exam-style source material: a short news article about a market (e.g., "avocado prices surge after poor harvest"), followed by questions requiring students to identify shifts, movements, and demand factors. No new theory.

## Chapter 3: Supply & Cost Structures

### §1 — Supply curve, supply factors, shifts 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> (a) A manufacturer of solar panels faces higher silicon prices. Show in a graph what happens to the supply curve for solar panels. Explain.
> (b) The government introduces a subsidy for solar panel production. Show the effect on the supply curve.
> (c) Name three factors that cause the supply curve to shift and give an example for each.
> (d) Draw a supply curve. Mark a movement along the curve and a shift of the curve. Label clearly which is which.

**Lesson goals:**
- Draw and interpret a supply curve (upward sloping)
- Name supply factors: input prices, technology, number of suppliers, expectations, government policy
- Distinguish between shifts and movements along the supply curve
- Show supply shifts graphically

**Difficulty notes:** Mirror of Ch2§2. Students pick this up faster because they have the demand framework. The shift/movement distinction gets reinforced. Exercise (c) deliberately does not give away the answer about own price — if a student lists "own price" as a shift factor, it reveals they have not grasped the distinction between movement and shift. This diagnostic value is important. One lesson comfortable.

---

### §2 — Cost structures: TK, VK, CK, GTK, GVK, GCK 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A bakery has fixed costs (constante kosten) of €500 per month (rent, insurance). Each loaf of bread costs €0.80 in ingredients and energy (variabele kosten per stuk).
> (a) Write the formulas for TK, TVK, and TCK as a function of Q.
> (b) Calculate TK, TVK, TCK at Q = 500 and Q = 1000.
> (c) Calculate GTK, GVK, and GCK at Q = 500 and Q = 1000.
> (d) What happens to GTK as Q increases? Explain why this is logical.
> (e) A student says: "GCK is always the same." Is this correct? Explain.

**Lesson goals:**
- Distinguish between vaste/constante kosten (CK) and variabele kosten (VK)
- Calculate TK = TCK + TVK
- Calculate averages: GTK = TK/Q, GVK = TVK/Q, GCK = TCK/Q
- Understand that GCK decreases as Q increases (spreading fixed costs)
- Understand that GVK is constant when VK per unit is constant

**Difficulty notes:** Seven cost concepts is a lot of terminology. The key insight (GTK falls because fixed costs spread) is intuitive. Exercises (d) and (e) test conceptual understanding, not just calculation. One lesson works because the formulas are all simple divisions — the challenge is keeping the abbreviations straight. Recommend a summary card students keep in their notebook.

---

### §3 — Revenue: TO, GO, winst, break-even ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> The bakery from §2 sells bread at €1.50 per loaf.
> (a) Write the formula for TO as a function of Q.
> (b) Calculate TO and winst (TO − TK) at Q = 500 and Q = 1000.
> (c) Calculate GO (= TO/Q) at Q = 500. What do you notice?
> (d) At which production level does the bakery exactly break even (winst = 0)? Show this with a calculation.
> (e) Draw a graph with TK and TO. Mark the break-even point. Shade the area of winst when Q = 1000.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate TO = P × Q
- Calculate GO = TO/Q (and notice GO = P at constant price)
- Calculate winst = TO − TK
- Find the break-even point algebraically (TO = TK)
- Show TK and TO in a graph and identify winst/verlies zones

**Difficulty notes:** Lighter than §2 because fewer new concepts. The break-even calculation is straightforward algebra. The graph (exercise e) bridges to visual analysis. One lesson comfortable — good opportunity to check that §2's cost concepts stuck.

---

### §4 — Consolidation 🟨

Integrative exercises combining costs and revenues. Include: "at which Q is winst maximized?" using tables (NOT yet marginal analysis — that comes in Ch4). Include a short text-based context (e.g., a bakery owner's annual report) with a table of costs and revenues, requiring students to extract and calculate. Build intuition for "there's a sweet spot."

## Chapter 4: Market Equilibrium & Marginal Analysis

*Note: this chapter has 4 theory paragraphs + consolidation (5 total) because marginal analysis requires two separate lessons — one to build the concepts, one to apply the profit maximisation rule.*

### §1 — Equilibrium: solving Qa = Qv 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> On the market for notebooks: Qv = −2P + 100 and Qa = 3P − 25.
> (a) Calculate the equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity.
> (b) Check your answer by substituting back into both equations.
> (c) Draw both curves in a graph and mark the equilibrium point.
> (d) At a price of €30, is there a surplus (aanbodoverschot) or a shortage (vraagoverschot)? Calculate its size.

**Lesson goals:**
- Set Qa = Qv and solve for P (equilibrium price)
- Substitute back to find equilibrium quantity
- Draw demand and supply curves and locate equilibrium graphically
- Determine surplus or shortage at a given price

**Difficulty notes:** First real algebraic challenge. Students who struggle with rearranging equations need support. Exercise (b) — checking the answer — builds good habits. Exercise (d) bridges to government intervention (Book 2). One lesson is tight if algebra is weak — provide a worked example first, then let students practice.

---

### §2 — Shifts in supply and demand, new equilibria 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Starting from the notebook market (Qv = −2P + 100, Qa = 3P − 25, equilibrium P* = 25, Q* = 50):
> (a) A new factory opens. The new supply function is Qa = 3P − 10. Calculate the new equilibrium price and quantity.
> (b) Compare with the old equilibrium: what happened to price? To quantity? Is this logical?
> (c) Now suppose student income rises. The new demand function is Qv = −2P + 120 (supply back to original). Calculate the new equilibrium.
> (d) Draw a graph showing both changes (one at a time). Use arrows to show the direction of shifts.
> (e) Now suppose BOTH changes happen at the same time: supply shifts to Qa = 3P − 10 AND demand shifts to Qv = −2P + 120. Calculate the new equilibrium. What happened to quantity? What happened to price? Could you have predicted the direction of the price change without calculating? Explain.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate a new equilibrium after a supply or demand shift
- Compare old and new equilibrium: predict direction of price and quantity change
- Connect shifts to the demand/supply factors from Ch2 and Ch3
- Show shifts graphically with clear before/after
- Analyse simultaneous shifts: quantity effect is clear (both increase Q), but price effect is ambiguous (one pushes up, the other pushes down)

**Difficulty notes:** Exercise (e) is the key diagnostic question. When both curves shift right, quantity unambiguously increases, but the price change depends on the relative size of the shifts — it could go up, down, or stay the same. Students who understand the model grasp this; students who are just plugging numbers are surprised. This is an important conceptual checkpoint. One lesson works — the algebra is revision from §1, the challenge is the reasoning in (e).

---

### §3 — Marginal concepts: MK and MO 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A firm has TK = 200 + 3Q (costs in €, Q in units) and sells at a fixed price of €8 per unit, so TO = 8Q.
> (a) Fill in the table:
>
> | Q   | TK  | TO  | Winst | MK (= ΔTK when Q rises by 1) | MO (= ΔTO when Q rises by 1) |
> |-----|-----|-----|-------|------|------|
> | 0   |     |     |       |  —   |  —   |
> | 10  |     |     |       |      |      |
> | 20  |     |     |       |      |      |
> | 30  |     |     |       |      |      |
> | 40  |     |     |       |      |      |
> | 50  |     |     |       |      |      |
>
> (b) What do you notice about MK? Is it constant or changing?
> (c) What do you notice about MO? Why is this logical given a constant price?
> (d) A second firm has TK = 100 + Q² and sells at €30 per unit. Fill in a similar table for Q = 0, 5, 10, 15, 20. What happens to MK now?
> (e) Explain in your own words: what does MK tell you? What does MO tell you?

**Lesson goals:**
- Define MK as the additional cost of producing one extra unit (ΔTK/ΔQ)
- Define MO as the additional revenue from selling one extra unit (ΔTO/ΔQ)
- Calculate MK and MO from a table using subtraction
- Observe that MK is constant for linear TK, but increasing for quadratic TK
- Observe that MO equals the price when price is constant
- Build intuitive understanding of marginal thinking

**Difficulty notes:** This paragraph introduces the concepts and calculation only — NOT yet the profit maximisation rule. Students work with tables and subtraction, discovering patterns. No differentiation yet. The quadratic cost function in exercise (d) prepares students for rising MK, which they will encounter in Book 3. One lesson works well because the focus is on understanding, not on a complex procedure.

---

### §4 — Profit maximisation: the MO = MK rule 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using Firm 2 from §3 (TK = 100 + Q², TO = 30Q):
> (a) Extend your table from §3(d) and add a column for winst at each Q.
> (b) At which Q is winst highest?
> (c) Look at your MK and MO columns. At the profit-maximising Q, what is true about MK and MO?
> (d) Explain the rule: "produce as long as MO ≥ MK, stop when MO = MK."
> (e) A third firm has TK = 50 + 2Q² and sells at P = 40. Using the rule MO = MK, calculate the profit-maximising Q WITHOUT making a full table. (Hint: MK = ΔTK/ΔQ. For TK = 50 + 2Q², each extra unit costs approximately 4Q. So set 4Q = 40.)
> (f) Verify your answer from (e) by calculating winst at Q-1, Q, and Q+1. Is winst indeed highest at your answer?

**Lesson goals:**
- Discover through the table that winst is maximised where MO = MK
- State the profit maximisation rule: produce where MO = MK
- Understand why: if MO > MK, one more unit adds to profit; if MO < MK, it subtracts
- Apply the rule directly (without a full table) by setting MO = MK as an equation
- Verify the solution by checking profit at nearby quantities

**Difficulty notes:** Exercise (e) is the bridge to algebraic application. The hint deliberately uses an approximate MK ("each extra unit costs approximately 4Q") rather than formal differentiation — that comes in Book 3. Students learn the logic of the rule here; the calculus technique comes later. Exercise (f) builds verification habits. One lesson works because the rule emerges naturally from the table work in §3.

---

### §5 — Consolidation 🟨

Full market equilibrium exercises: given functions, find equilibrium, calculate costs/revenue/profit, apply marginal rule. Include exam-style context: a short text about a fruit stand at a market with a cost table, requiring students to find the profit-maximising output and explain their reasoning. Combine graph reading with calculation.

---

## Chapter 5: Test Preparation

Practice exam covering all Chapter 1–4 content. This prepares students for the 120-minute end-of-book test. Include graph drawing, calculations, and short explanation questions. Use longer text-based exam contexts with data tables and graphs as sources. Note: the 45-minute mid-book test comes after Chapter 2 and covers Chapters 1–2 only.

# BOOK 2 — Elasticities, Surplus & Welfare

## Chapter 1: Elasticities

### §1 — Price elasticity: concept and calculation 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A cinema raises ticket prices from €10.00 to €12.00. The number of visitors drops from 500 to 420 per week.
> (a) Calculate the percentage change in price.
> (b) Calculate the percentage change in quantity demanded.
> (c) Calculate the price elasticity of demand (Ev = %ΔQv / %ΔP).
> (d) Is the demand for cinema tickets elastic or inelastic? Explain what this means in plain language.
> (e) A petrol station raises its price from €1.80 to €1.90. Sales drop from 10,000 to 9,800 litres. Calculate Ev. Is petrol elastic or inelastic? Why does this make sense?

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate percentage changes for price and quantity
- Apply the formula Ev = %ΔQv / %ΔP
- Classify demand as elastic (|Ev| > 1) or inelastic (|Ev| < 1)
- Explain intuitively why some goods are more elastic than others (substitutes, necessity vs luxury)

**Difficulty notes:** The formula itself is simple, but students make errors in the percentage calculation (using new instead of old as base, forgetting the sign). Exercise (e) provides immediate contrast — elastic vs inelastic — which cements understanding. One lesson is doable if the percentage skill from Book 1 Ch1§2 is solid. If not, this spills.

---

### §2 — Elastic vs inelastic: the connection to omzet (revenue) 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the cinema from §1 (price up from €10 to €12, visitors down from 500 to 420):
> (a) Calculate omzet (total revenue = P × Q) before and after the price increase.
> (b) Did omzet go up or down? Explain this using the elasticity you calculated.
> (c) The cinema owner wants to maximise omzet. Based on the Ev, should he raise the price further or lower it? Explain the rule.
> (d) Now consider the petrol station (price up from €1.80 to €1.90, litres from 10,000 to 9,800). Calculate omzet before and after. Did the price increase help the petrol station's revenue? Why?
> (e) Complete the rule: "If demand is elastic, a price increase leads to … omzet. If demand is inelastic, a price increase leads to … omzet."

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate omzet = P × Q before and after a price change
- Explain that elastic demand means quantity drops proportionally more than price rises → omzet falls
- Explain that inelastic demand means quantity drops proportionally less → omzet rises
- Apply the rule to advise a business on pricing strategy

**Difficulty notes:** This is where elasticity becomes meaningful — students see why businesses care. Note that the formula omzet = P × Q (total revenue) is used here for the first time this year in a formal way. Students have an intuitive notion of revenue, but this is the first time they apply the formula systematically and connect it to a concept (elasticity). The rule "elastic → price up means omzet down" is counterintuitive for some. One lesson works — the calculations are simple repetitions of §1, the new element is the interpretation.

---

### §3 — Income elasticity and cross elasticity 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> *Part A — Income elasticity:*
> (a) Average household income rises by 5%. Demand for organic food rises by 8%. Demand for budget instant noodles drops by 3%.
> Calculate the income elasticity (Ei) for organic food and for instant noodles.
> Classify each: normal good or inferior good? Luxury or necessity?
>
> *Part B — Cross elasticity:*
> (b) The price of coffee rises by 10%. The demand for tea rises by 4%. The demand for coffee filters drops by 6%.
> Calculate the cross elasticity (Ek) for tea with respect to coffee price, and for coffee filters with respect to coffee price.
> Classify: are tea and coffee substitutes or complements? What about coffee and coffee filters?
>
> *Part C — Demand functions with multiple variables:*
> (c) The demand for good X is given by: Qx = 100 − 2Px + 0.5Py + 0.01Y, where Px is the price of X, Py is the price of good Y, and Y is income.
> Currently: Px = 10, Py = 20, Y = 30,000.
> Calculate the current quantity demanded for X.
> Income rises to Y = 33,000 (other factors unchanged). Calculate the new Qd. Calculate Ei.
> The price of Y rises from 20 to 24 (other factors at original values). Calculate the new Qd. Calculate Ek. Are X and Y substitutes or complements?
>
> (d) Using the demand function from (c): predict what happens to the demand for X if a recession lowers income by 10%. Calculate the new Qd.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate Ei = %ΔQd / %ΔIncome
- Classify goods: normal (Ei > 0) vs inferior (Ei < 0), necessity (0 < Ei < 1) vs luxury (Ei > 1)
- Calculate Ek = %ΔQd(good A) / %ΔP(good B)
- Classify pairs: substitutes (Ek > 0) vs complements (Ek < 0)
- Read and use a demand function with multiple variables (price of other goods, income)
- Use elasticity for predictions

**Difficulty notes:** Parts A and B use the same formula structure as Ev — same skill, different variables. Students who mastered §1 handle this quickly. Part C introduces multi-variable demand functions, which appear in exam questions. Students must be comfortable substituting multiple values and holding other variables constant (ceteris paribus in action). This is the most novel element. One lesson works because parts A and B are fast; part C takes more time but is essential for exam preparation.

---

### §4 — Consolidation 🟨

Mixed elasticity problems with exam-style source material. Include a table of real market data (e.g., CBS data on consumer spending) and ask students to calculate relevant elasticities. Include: "given this Ev, what happens to omzet if price changes by X%?" Include one exercise with a multi-variable demand function in a news context. Include a question where students must determine which elasticity to calculate from context.

## Chapter 2: Consumer Surplus, Producer Surplus & Pareto-efficiency

### §1 — Consumer surplus 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The demand curve for concert tickets is P = 50 − 0.5Q (P in €, Q in tickets).
> The market price is €20.
> (a) Calculate the equilibrium quantity demanded at this price.
> (b) Draw the demand curve. Shade the area that represents the consumer surplus.
> (c) Calculate the consumer surplus. (Hint: it is a triangle.)
> (d) Explain in your own words what consumer surplus means. Why is it a measure of welfare for buyers?

**Lesson goals:**
- Define consumer surplus as the difference between willingness to pay and actual price, summed over all buyers
- Identify CS graphically as the triangle between the demand curve and the price line
- Calculate CS as area of a triangle: ½ × base × height
- Interpret CS as a measure of buyer welfare

**Difficulty notes:** The concept is visual and concrete. The calculation (triangle area) is simple geometry. The main stumbling block is correctly identifying which triangle — students sometimes shade the wrong area. One lesson comfortable.

---

### §2 — Producer surplus, total surplus, and the link to marginal cost 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The supply curve for concert tickets is P = 5 + 0.25Q. The demand curve is P = 50 − 0.5Q.
> (a) Calculate the equilibrium price and quantity.
> (b) Draw both curves. Shade and label CS and PS in different colours.
> (c) Calculate CS and PS separately.
> (d) Calculate the total surplus (TS = CS + PS).
> (e) The supply curve represents the marginal cost of each additional ticket. Explain why producer surplus can also be read as: "the difference between the price received and the marginal cost of production, summed over all units sold." Show this in your graph.
> (f) A promoter asks: "Is this the best possible outcome for society?" Answer using the concept of total surplus.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define producer surplus as the difference between actual price received and minimum acceptable price, summed over all sellers
- Identify PS graphically (triangle between price line and supply curve)
- Calculate PS as a triangle area
- Calculate total surplus = CS + PS
- Understand that the supply curve IS the marginal cost curve — so PS is the area between price and MC
- Begin to understand that competitive equilibrium maximises total surplus

**Difficulty notes:** The graphical work for PS mirrors CS — students pick it up quickly. The challenging element is exercise (e): connecting PS to marginal cost. The supply curve has been treated as "willingness to sell" so far; now students must see it as the MC curve. This dual interpretation (supply = MC) is critical for understanding welfare effects of monopoly in Book 3. Spend time on this connection — it is more important than the triangle calculation itself. One lesson works if you prioritise the MC connection over drilling calculations.

---

### §3 — Pareto-efficiency and deadweight loss introduction 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the market from §2 (equilibrium P = €20, Q = 60, TS = 1350):
> (a) Suppose the government forces the price to €30. Calculate the new Qd and Qs. Which one determines actual transactions? Why?
> (b) Calculate CS and PS at the forced price of €30.
> (c) Calculate the new total surplus. How much surplus is lost compared to the free market equilibrium?
> (d) The lost surplus is called deadweight loss (welvaartsverlies). Shade it in your graph.
> (e) Explain in your own words what Pareto-efficient means. Why is the free market equilibrium Pareto-efficient and the situation at P = €30 not?

**Lesson goals:**
- Define Pareto-efficiency: no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off
- Understand that the competitive equilibrium is Pareto-efficient (TS is maximised)
- Calculate deadweight loss when price deviates from equilibrium
- Recognise that any price above or below equilibrium creates deadweight loss
- Identify deadweight loss graphically (the "lost triangle")

**Difficulty notes:** The Pareto concept is abstract but exercise (e) makes it concrete: "can you improve someone's position without hurting another?" The deadweight loss calculation is straightforward (it's another triangle). The conceptual challenge is that students must see why transactions are "lost." One lesson works because the math is revision — the new element is the interpretation.

### §4 — Consolidation 🟨

Practice with different demand/supply functions. Include exam-style source material: a graph with demand and supply curves and a policy intervention, requiring students to calculate surplus and welfare loss. Include: "rank three market outcomes by total surplus." Include a verbal explanation question: "a politician says a maximum price helps consumers. Evaluate this claim using surplus analysis."

## Chapter 3: Government Intervention — Taxes & Subsidies

### §1 — Taxes: the wedge and the new equilibrium 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Market: Qv = −2P + 20 and Qa = P − 1. The government introduces a tax of €3 per unit, paid by the producer.
> (a) Calculate the original equilibrium price and quantity.
> (b) The tax shifts the supply curve upward by €3. Write the new supply function.
> (c) Calculate the new equilibrium. What is the consumer price (what the buyer pays)? What is the producer price (what the seller keeps after paying tax)?
> (d) Draw the situation before and after the tax. Mark the tax wedge.

**Lesson goals:**
- Understand that a per-unit tax creates a wedge between consumer and producer price
- Calculate the new supply function after tax (shift supply up by tax amount)
- Calculate the new equilibrium with tax
- Distinguish between consumer price and producer price
- Show the tax wedge graphically

**Difficulty notes:** The concept of "the supply curve shifts up by the tax amount" is often taught but rarely understood. Students confuse "upward shift = less supply." Exercise (d) is essential — the graph makes the wedge visible. One lesson works if you focus on the mechanics (how to calculate) and save the welfare analysis for §2.

### §2 — Taxes: burden sharing, afwenteling and welfare loss 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the market from §1 (original: P* = 7, Q* = 6; after tax €3: Pc = 8, Pp = 5, Qt = 3):
> (a) How much of the €3 tax is paid by the consumer? How much by the producer?
> (b) Calculate the afwentelingspercentage (% of tax passed on to consumer).
> (c) Calculate CS before and after the tax.
> (d) Calculate PS before and after the tax.
> (e) Calculate total tax revenue for the government.
> (f) Calculate the deadweight loss. Verify: old TS = new CS + new PS + tax revenue + DWL.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate how the tax burden is shared between buyer and seller
- Calculate the afwentelingspercentage
- Calculate CS, PS and tax revenue in the post-tax situation
- Calculate deadweight loss from taxation
- Understand that taxes always create some deadweight loss (except in extreme elasticity cases)

**Difficulty notes:** This is a calculation-heavy lesson with 5–6 separate computations. The key insight — "not all lost CS and PS goes to the government; some is simply lost" — is the deadweight loss. Students need the graph from §1 in front of them. One lesson is tight. Recommend: do exercises (a–d) in class, assign (e–f) as homework.

### §3 — Subsidies: mirror of taxes 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Market: Qv = −4P + 40 and Qa = 2P − 2. The government gives a subsidy of €3 per unit to producers.
> (a) Calculate the original equilibrium.
> (b) Write the new supply function (supply shifts down by subsidy amount).
> (c) Calculate the new equilibrium. What price does the consumer pay? What does the producer effectively receive?
> (d) Calculate CS and PS before and after the subsidy.
> (e) Calculate the total cost of the subsidy to the government (= subsidy × new Q).
> (f) Calculate the deadweight loss. (Yes, subsidies also create DWL.)
> (g) Draw the complete graph showing CS, PS, government cost, and DWL.

**Lesson goals:**
- Apply the tax framework in reverse: supply shifts down instead of up
- Calculate new equilibrium, consumer and producer prices after subsidy
- Calculate CS, PS, government expenditure, and DWL
- Understand that subsidies also create deadweight loss (overproduction beyond efficient level)

**Difficulty notes:** If students understood §1 and §2, this is mechanical transfer — same skills, different direction. The surprise is that subsidies also cause DWL (students assume subsidies are "purely good"). One lesson works because the method is identical to taxes.

### §1 — Maximum price (prijsplafond) 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**

- Calculate welfare effects: some CS is gained from lower price, some is lost from fewer transactions
- Identify deadweight loss graphically and computationally
- Evaluate the trade-off: lower price for some vs fewer available units

**Difficulty notes:** The welfare analysis here is more nuanced than taxes: some consumers gain, others lose access entirely. Exercise (f) forces critical thinking. One lesson works — the graph is similar to Ch2§3 (forced price deviation), so students recognise the pattern.

### §2 — Minimum price (minimumprijs/garantieprijs) 🟨 MEDIUM

**Difficulty notes:** Mirror of §1. Students who understood maximum price handle this quickly. The surplus (aanbodoverschot) vs shortage distinction is important to drill. The EU butter/milk example makes it concrete. One lesson comfortable.

## Chapter 1: Perfect Competition

### §1 — Characteristics of volkomen concurrentie ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> A wheat farmer sells wheat at the market price of €200 per ton. He cannot influence this price.
> (a) Name four characteristics of a perfectly competitive market. For each, explain why the wheat market fits.
> (b) The farmer considers charging €210 per ton. Explain why this won't work.
> (c) Draw the farmer's individual price line. Label it P = GO = MO.
> (d) Explain why for this farmer MO = price.

**Lesson goals:**
- Name the four characteristics: many buyers and sellers, homogeneous product, free entry/exit, perfect information
- Explain why individual firms are price takers (hoeveelheidsaanpasser)
- Understand that P = GO = MO in perfect competition (horizontal price line)
- Draw the individual firm's price/revenue lines

**Difficulty notes:** Conceptual lesson. The P = GO = MO equality is stated and understood visually, not derived mathematically. Students find this intuitive ("if you always get the same price, each extra unit brings in that price"). One lesson comfortable — use the extra time for real-world examples (commodity markets, agriculture).

---

### §2 — Profit maximisation: MO = MK at volkomen concurrentie 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The wheat farmer's costs are TK = 0.5Q² + 10Q + 500 (Q in tons). Market price is €200/ton.
> (a) Write down the MK function. (Hint: differentiate TK. Remember: if TK = aQ² + bQ + c, then MK = 2aQ + b.)
> (b) Write down the MO. (What is MO for a price taker?)
> (c) Set MO = MK and solve for Q. This is the profit-maximising quantity.
> (d) Calculate total revenue, total costs, and total profit at this Q.
> (e) Draw a graph with MK and the horizontal price/MO line. Mark the profit-maximising point.
> (f) Would this farmer produce at Q = 100? Explain using MO and MK.

**Lesson goals:**
- Differentiate a simple quadratic TK function to find MK (power rule: bring down exponent, reduce by one)
- State MO = P for a price-taking firm
- Find profit-maximising output by setting MO = MK
- Calculate winst at optimal Q
- Show the MO = MK solution graphically
- Explain why producing beyond MO = MK reduces profit

**Difficulty notes:** This is where differentiation enters the course. The power rule on a quadratic is simple ("multiply by 2, drop the Q's power by 1"). Students need to see it as a recipe. The conceptual link ("marginal cost tells you what one more ton costs") was established in Book 1 Ch4§3–§4. Exercise (f) tests whether they understand the rule, not just apply it. One lesson is tight — spend the first 15 minutes on the differentiation technique, then apply it.

---

### §3 — Long-run equilibrium: entry and exit 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The wheat farmer from §2 makes a profit.
> (a) Explain what will happen in the long run: will new farmers enter or exit the market? What happens to total market supply?
> (b) Show in a market diagram (supply and demand) what happens to the market price when supply increases.
> (c) At what profit level will entry stop? Explain.
> (d) In long-run equilibrium, price equals the minimum of GTK. Using TK = 0.5Q² + 10Q + 500, write the formula for GTK. Set GTK = MK (= Q + 10) and solve for Q. This is the efficient scale of the firm.
> (e) Calculate GTK at the Q you found in (d). This is the long-run equilibrium price. Verify that profit = 0 at this price.
> (f) Draw the long-run equilibrium for the individual firm: P = GTK at the minimum of GTK, with the MK curve passing through that point. Explain why this is the long-run result.

**Lesson goals:**
- Explain that positive profit attracts entry; negative profit causes exit
- Understand that entry shifts market supply right → price falls
- Understand that in long-run equilibrium: economic profit = 0
- Calculate the efficient scale by setting GTK = MK and solving for Q
- Calculate the long-run equilibrium price from the minimum GTK
- Show that long-run equilibrium occurs where P = minimum GTK = MK
- Draw the individual firm's long-run equilibrium graph

**Difficulty notes:** Conceptual reasoning about dynamics plus a meaningful calculation. Exercise (d) is the key new skill: setting GTK = MK. Students write GTK = TK/Q = 0.5Q + 10 + 500/Q, then set this equal to MK = Q + 10, giving 0.5Q + 10 + 500/Q = Q + 10, which simplifies to 500/Q = 0.5Q, so Q² = 1000, Q ≈ 31.6. This algebra is demanding — the 500/Q term is unfamiliar. Guide students through it step by step. Exercise (e) provides the satisfying verification (profit = 0). One lesson works if you prioritise the calculation and keep the graphical work in (f) concise.

### §2 — MO at monopoly: understanding the declining MO curve 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A monopolist faces demand Qv = 100 − 2P, or equivalently P = 50 − 0.5Q.
> (a) Complete the table:
>
> | Q  | P    | TO (=P×Q) | MO (=ΔTO/ΔQ) |
> |----|------|-----------|---------------|
> | 0  |      |           |               |
> | 10 |      |           |               |
> | 20 |      |           |               |
> | 30 |      |           |               |
> | 40 |      |           |               |
> | 50 |      |           |               |
> | 60 |      |           |               |
>
> (b) At which Q is TO maximised? What is MO at that point?
> (c) Draw the demand curve (P line) and the MO curve in the same graph. What do you notice about the slope of MO compared to the demand curve?
> (d) Explain in words why MO is lower than the price for a monopolist. (Hint: to sell one more unit, the monopolist must lower the price on ALL units.)
> (e) Verify using the formula: TO = P × Q = (50 − 0.5Q) × Q = 50Q − 0.5Q². Differentiate to find MO. Compare with your table.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate TO from a demand function: TO = P × Q
- Calculate MO from a table (ΔTO/ΔQ)
- Discover that MO < P for a monopolist (because lowering price on all units)

- Derive MO by differentiating TO (first application of power rule on a quadratic revenue function)

**Difficulty notes:** This is the key preparation lesson for §3. The table method (exercises a–c) builds intuition BEFORE the algebra (exercise e). Students see the pattern in the numbers first, then verify with calculus. The differentiation in (e) uses the same power rule from Ch1§2. One lesson works IF you do the table first — don't start with algebra.

### §3 — Profit maximisation at monopoly: full calculation 🟥→🟨 HEAVY (made manageable)

**Target exercise:**
> The monopolist from §2 has demand P = 50 − 0.5Q and costs TK = 0.25Q² + 5Q + 100.
> Step-by-step:
> (a) Write TO as a function of Q: TO = (50 − 0.5Q) × Q. Expand.
> (b) Differentiate TO to find MO.
> (c) Differentiate TK to find MK.
> (d) Set MO = MK. Solve for Q.
> (e) Find the price by substituting Q back into the demand function P = 50 − 0.5Q.
> (f) Calculate total profit: Winst = TO − TK.
> (g) Draw the graph: demand curve, MO, MK. Mark the profit-maximising Q and P. Shade the area of profit.
> (h) Could the monopolist charge a higher price than your answer in (e)? What would happen to quantity and profit?

**Lesson goals:**
- Execute the full monopoly profit maximisation procedure:
  1. Derive TO from demand function
  2. Differentiate TO → MO
  3. Differentiate TK → MK
  4. Solve MO = MK → optimal Q
  5. Substitute back into demand → optimal P
  6. Calculate winst
- Draw the complete monopoly graph with MO, MK, demand curve
- Identify the profit area graphically

**Difficulty notes:** The most demanding lesson in the course. 6 algebraic steps plus a graph. However, students have been prepared: MK differentiation (Ch1§2), MO table and differentiation (Ch2§2), MO = MK rule (Book 1 Ch4§3–§4). The exercise is scaffolded step-by-step. In class: work through steps (a–f) together as guided practice, assign (g–h) as homework. One lesson is tight but feasible with this scaffolding.

## Chapter 4: Market Failure & External Effects

### §1 — Negative external effects 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A factory produces chemicals. Private marginal costs are MPC = 2Q. But production causes water pollution costing nearby fishermen €4 per unit. So social marginal costs are MSC = 2Q + 4. Market demand is P = 20 − Q.
> (a) Calculate the market equilibrium (where demand = MPC). This is what the market produces without government intervention.
> (b) Calculate the socially optimal equilibrium (where demand = MSC).
> (c) Draw both equilibria in one graph. Shade the welfare loss from overproduction.
> (d) How many units too many does the free market produce?
> (e) Explain why this is called market failure. Who bears the cost that the market ignores?

**Lesson goals:**
- Define external effects (externalities): costs/benefits affecting third parties not involved in the transaction
- Distinguish private costs from social costs: MSC = MPC + external cost
- Calculate market equilibrium (using MPC) and socially optimal equilibrium (using MSC)
- Calculate the welfare loss from overproduction (the market produces too much)
- Explain why externalities cause market failure: the market price doesn't reflect the full cost

**Difficulty notes:** New graph type: two supply curves (MPC and MSC). Students must understand why the market uses MPC (because firms don't pay for pollution) while society should use MSC. The math is straightforward — it's the same equilibrium calculation as Book 1, just done twice. One lesson works.

---

### §2 — Positive external effects 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Vaccinations have positive external effects: each person vaccinated protects others (herd immunity). Private marginal benefits are MPB. Social marginal benefits are MSB = MPB + €5 (the benefit to others).
> Demand (= MPB): P = 30 − Q. Supply (= MC): P = Q.
> (a) Calculate the market equilibrium (where supply = MPB).
> (b) Calculate the socially optimal equilibrium (where supply = MSB). To do this: MSB = 30 − Q + 5 = 35 − Q. Set equal to supply.
> (c) The market produces too little. By how many units?
> (d) Draw both equilibria. Shade the welfare loss from underproduction.
> (e) Suggest a policy to correct this market failure. How large should the correction be?

**Lesson goals:**
- Distinguish private benefits from social benefits: MSB = MPB + external benefit
- Calculate market equilibrium (using MPB) and socially optimal equilibrium (using MSB)
- Understand that the market produces too little of goods with positive externalities
- Suggest correction: subsidy equal to the external benefit per unit
- Draw the graph with MPB and MSB

**Difficulty notes:** Mirror of §1 — if students understood negative externalities, this is the same logic reversed. The policy suggestion (exercise e) connects back to Book 2 Ch3 (subsidies). One lesson comfortable.

### §3 — Government correction of market failure 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**

**Lesson goals:**

- Understand the Coase theorem: private negotiation can solve externalities if transaction costs are low

# BOOK 4 — Labor Markets, Trade & Macro Basics

## Chapter 1: Labor Market Basics

### §1 — Labor demand: derived demand and marginal productivity ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> A restaurant hires waiters. Each waiter can serve a certain number of additional meals per evening:
>
> | Waiter # | Extra meals served (marginal product) | Revenue per meal |
> |----------|--------------------------------------|-----------------|
> | 1st      | 50                                   | €15             |
> | 2nd      | 45                                   | €15             |
> | 3rd      | 35                                   | €15             |
> | 4th      | 25                                   | €15             |
> | 5th      | 15                                   | €15             |
>
> (a) Calculate the value of marginal product (VMP = marginal product × revenue per meal) for each waiter.
> (b) The wage is €450 per evening. How many waiters should the restaurant hire? Explain using the VMP.
> (c) The wage rises to €600. How many waiters now? What happened to labor demand?
> (d) Explain why the demand for labor is called "derived demand."
> (e) Draw the restaurant's labor demand curve using the VMP values.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define marginal product of labor (extra output from one additional worker)
- Calculate value of marginal product: VMP = MP × price of output
- Apply the hiring rule: hire as long as VMP ≥ wage
- Understand derived demand: labor is demanded because of demand for the product
- Draw a labor demand curve (which is the VMP curve)

**Difficulty notes:** Connects directly to marginal analysis from Book 1 and Book 3 (MO = MK is analogous to VMP = W). Students find the restaurant context engaging. The diminishing marginal product is intuitive ("the kitchen gets crowded"). One lesson comfortable.

### §2 — Labor supply and participation rate 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Country X has the following data:
> - Total population aged 15–75: 12 million
> - People in the beroepsbevolking (labor force): 9 million
> - Werkloze beroepsbevolking (unemployed, seeking work): 540,000
>
> (a) Calculate the bruto participatiegraad (participation rate).
> (b) Calculate the werkloosheidspercentage (unemployment rate).
> (c) A student says: "3 million people are unemployed because 12 − 9 = 3 million." Explain why this is wrong.
> (d) Average wages rise significantly. Explain how this might affect the participation rate (think about people currently in the niet-beroepsbevolking).
> (e) Draw a labor supply curve. Explain why it is upward sloping.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define beroepsbevolking, niet-beroepsbevolking, werkloze beroepsbevolking
- Calculate participation rate = beroepsbevolking / population 15–75 × 100
- Calculate unemployment rate = werklozen / beroepsbevolking × 100
- Explain what drives labor supply: wages, preferences, demographics
- Draw an upward-sloping labor supply curve

**Difficulty notes:** The terminology (beroepsbevolking vs niet-beroepsbevolking) is the main challenge — students mix up "not working" with "unemployed." Exercise (c) directly addresses this confusion. The formulas are simple. One lesson works with clear emphasis on definitions.

### §3 — Labor market equilibrium ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> The labor market for IT workers: Qd = −200W + 5000 and Qs = 300W − 1000 (W = wage in €/hour, Q = number of workers).

> (b) At a wage of €14/hour, is there a krappe arbeidsmarkt or a ruime arbeidsmarkt? Calculate the gap.
> (c) In a krappe arbeidsmarkt, what happens to wages? In a ruime arbeidsmarkt? Explain.
> (d) Draw the labor market diagram with the equilibrium and the situation at W = €14.
> (e) New tech companies enter the market, increasing demand for IT workers. The new demand is Qd = −200W + 7000. Calculate the new equilibrium. What happened to wages and employment?

**Lesson goals:**
- Apply supply-demand equilibrium framework to the labor market
- Calculate equilibrium wage and employment
- Define krappe arbeidsmarkt (demand > supply) and ruime arbeidsmarkt (supply > demand)
- Predict wage adjustments from market imbalances
- Analyse shifts in labor demand or supply

**Difficulty notes:** Direct transfer from Book 1 Ch4 — same math, different context. Students recognise the framework. Exercise (e) mirrors Book 1 Ch4§2. One lesson comfortable. This is a confidence-building lesson.

## Chapter 2: Wages, Minimum Wage & Unemployment

### §1 — Types of unemployment 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Classify each situation as conjuncturele werkloosheid, structurele werkloosheid, or frictiewerkloosheid. Explain your choice.
> (a) A coal miner in Limburg loses his job because the mine closes permanently due to the shift to renewable energy.
> (b) A marketing manager quits her job to search for a better position. She is unemployed for 3 months.
> (c) A construction worker is laid off because the housing market crashed during a recession.
> (d) Factory workers are replaced by robots. Their skills don't match available jobs.
> (e) For each type, explain whether it is temporary or can persist for a long time.
> (f) A government wants to reduce structurele werkloosheid. Name two policy measures and explain how they work.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define three types of unemployment: conjunctureel (cyclical), structureel (structural), frictie (frictional)
- Classify real-world cases into the correct type
- Understand causes: demand shortfall (cyclical), skills mismatch or automation (structural), search time (frictional)
- Suggest policy responses for each type
- Understand that frictional unemployment is normal and even healthy

**Difficulty notes:** Definitional lesson. The classification can be ambiguous (is the coal miner structural or frictional?) — this makes for good discussion. Exercise (f) requires policy thinking. One lesson works.

### §2 — Minimum wage: welfare effects on the labor market 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the IT labor market from Ch1§3: Qd = −200W + 5000, Qs = 300W − 1000 (equilibrium W* = €12, Q* = 2600).
> (a) The government sets a minimum wage of €15/hour. Calculate Qd and Qs at this wage.
> (b) How many workers become unemployed? (= Qs − Qd)
> (c) Draw the labor market with the minimum wage. Shade the werknemerssurplus and werkgeverssurplus.
> (d) Calculate the total surplus before and after the minimum wage.
> (e) Calculate the deadweight loss.
> (f) Some workers earn more (€15 instead of €12), but others lose their jobs. Evaluate: "A minimum wage helps workers." Use your calculations to argue both sides.

**Lesson goals:**
- Apply the minimum price framework from Book 2 Ch4§2 to the labor market
- Calculate unemployment caused by a minimum wage (surplus of labor)
- Calculate welfare effects: werknemerssurplus, werkgeverssurplus, deadweight loss
- Recognise the trade-off: higher wages for employed vs job losses for others
- Formulate a balanced evaluation of minimum wage policy

**Difficulty notes:** This is a direct transfer from Book 2 (minimumprijs). Students should recognise the framework: a minimum wage IS a minimum price on labor. The welfare calculation is identical. The new element is exercise (f) — a balanced evaluation — which is an exam skill (standpuntbepaling). One lesson works. If there is a long time gap between Book 2 and this lesson, start with a 10-minute revision of minimum price mechanics.

## Chapter 3: International Trade

### §1 — Absolute and comparative advantage 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Two countries produce cars and grain:
>
> | Country | Hours per car | Hours per ton of grain |
> |---------|--------------|----------------------|
> | A       | 100          | 10                   |
> | B       | 150          | 30                   |
>
> (a) Which country has an absolute advantage in cars? In grain?
> (b) Calculate the opportunity cost of 1 car in each country (in tons of grain given up).
> (c) Calculate the opportunity cost of 1 ton of grain in each country (in cars given up).
> (d) Which country has a comparative advantage in cars? In grain? Explain.
> (e) A student says: "Country A is better at everything, so trade doesn't make sense." Explain why this is wrong using your answers.

**Lesson goals:**
- Define absolute advantage (produce more with same resources)
- Define comparative advantage (lowest opportunity cost)
- Calculate opportunity costs from a production table
- Explain that comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, determines trade patterns
- Understand that trade benefits both countries even if one is better at everything

**Difficulty notes:** The opportunity cost calculation is the tricky part — students must think in terms of "what you give up." The counterintuitive result (B can have a comparative advantage despite being worse at everything) is why the concept is hard. One lesson is tight — allocate time for the "aha moment."

---

### §2 — Gains from trade, terms of trade 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using countries A and B from §1 (A: comparative advantage in grain, B: comparative advantage in cars):
> Before trade, each country uses 3000 labor hours total and splits evenly between products.
> (a) Calculate production of each good in each country before trade.
> (b) Now each country fully specialises. Calculate total world production of each good.
> (c) Show that world production has increased for at least one good (without decreasing the other).
> (d) Country A exports grain to B and imports cars. The terms of trade are: 1 car = 8 tons of grain. With trade, A consumes 15 cars and 185 tons of grain. Is A better off than before trade? Show with numbers.
> (e) At which terms of trade would A refuse to trade? At which would B refuse? (Hint: use opportunity costs from §1.)

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate production before and after specialisation
- Show that specialisation increases total world output
- Define terms of trade: the exchange ratio between goods
- Determine the range of mutually beneficial terms of trade (between the two opportunity costs)
- Understand that both countries can gain from trade

**Difficulty notes:** Lots of arithmetic but conceptually straightforward once comparative advantage is understood. Exercise (e) requires connecting back to opportunity costs — elegant but potentially confusing. One lesson works if §1 was solid. If not, this spills.

### §3 — Protectionism and trade policy 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Domestic market: Qv = −2P + 200, Qa = P − 10 (P in €, Q in thousands of units). The world price is €40.
> (a) At free trade: calculate domestic Qd, domestic Qa, and imports (Qd − Qa).
> (b) The government imposes a tariff (import tax) of €10. The new import price is €50. Calculate the new domestic Qd, Qa, and imports.
> (c) Calculate the tariff revenue for the government.
> (d) Draw the free-trade and post-tariff situations. Shade the two deadweight loss triangles.
> (e) Who benefits from the tariff? Who loses? Evaluate: "Tariffs protect domestic jobs."

**Lesson goals:**

- Calculate tariff revenue

**Difficulty notes:** The graph has two DWL triangles (production inefficiency + consumption loss) — this is more complex than the single DWL triangle from taxes. One lesson works because it's application, not new methodology.

## Chapter 4: Inflation, Purchasing Power & Prices

### §1 — Inflation, CPI, and price indices 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A shopping basket costs €150 in 2023 (base year, index = 100) and €162 in 2025.
> (a) Calculate the CPI for 2025.
> (b) Calculate the inflation rate from 2023 to 2025.
> (c) In 2026, the CPI is 112. Calculate the inflation from 2025 to 2026. (Watch out: not 4%!)
> (d) A worker's nominal wage rises from €3,000/month in 2023 to €3,200 in 2025. Calculate the real wage change. Did the worker's purchasing power increase or decrease?
> (e) Explain the difference between nominal and real values. Why do economists prefer to use real values?

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate CPI = (basket cost in year t / basket cost in base year) × 100
- Calculate inflation rate from CPI values
- Distinguish between index point changes and percentage changes (critical exam skill!)
- Calculate real values: real wage = nominal wage / CPI × 100
- Explain why real values correct for inflation

**Difficulty notes:** Exercise (c) is the classic trap — students think "108 to 112 = 4% inflation" when it's actually (112−108)/108 = 3.7%. This connects directly to Book 1 Ch1§2 (percentage changes). The real vs nominal distinction is conceptually important but mathematically simple. One lesson works.

---

### §2 — Purchasing power and indexering 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A pension is €1,500 per month. Inflation is 4%. Average wages rose by 6%.
> (a) The pension is waardevast (indexed to inflation). Calculate the new pension.
> (b) The pension is welvaartsvast (indexed to wage growth). Calculate the new pension.
> (c) The pension is bevroren (frozen). Calculate the real purchasing power loss in euros per month.
> (d) Which indexering method is best for the pensioner? Which is cheapest for the pension fund? Explain the trade-off.
> (e) A country experiences 2% deflation. What happens to the purchasing power of a frozen pension? Is this good or bad for the pensioner?

**Lesson goals:**
- Define waardevast (indexed to CPI/inflation) vs welvaartsvast (indexed to wages)
- Calculate pensions/wages after indexering
- Calculate purchasing power changes when pensions are frozen
- Understand the trade-off between protecting pensioners and pension fund sustainability
- Apply concepts to deflation (reverse situation)

**Difficulty notes:** The terminology (waardevast, welvaartsvast, bevroren) is confusing because the words sound similar. Exercises (a–c) force precise calculation for each case. Exercise (e) tests deeper understanding. One lesson works — the math is arithmetic, the challenge is keeping the definitions straight.

---

### §3 — Real vs nominal: integration and application 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> (a) The nominal interest rate on a savings account is 5%. Inflation is 3%. Calculate the real interest rate (use: real ≈ nominal − inflation). If you save €10,000, how much more can you actually buy after one year?
> (b) Country X's nominal GDP grew from €800 billion to €840 billion. The GDP deflator rose from 100 to 103. Calculate real GDP growth. Did the country produce more goods and services, or did prices just rise?
> (c) A worker's nominal wage rises from €2,500 to €2,600 (4% increase). Inflation is 5%. Calculate the real wage change. Is this worker better or worse off?
> (d) In the 1970s, inflation in the Netherlands was 10% and nominal wages rose by 8%. Explain what happened to workers' purchasing power. How might this affect consumption?
> (e) Combine your knowledge: explain why a central bank targets low inflation (around 2%). Use the concepts real interest rate, purchasing power, and wage earners in your answer.

**Lesson goals:**
- Calculate real interest rate ≈ nominal interest rate − inflation
- Calculate real GDP growth using the GDP deflator
- Apply the real vs nominal framework to wages, interest rates, and GDP
- Understand why persistent inflation erodes purchasing power
- Explain why central banks target low inflation (integration question)

**Difficulty notes:** This is an integration lesson — no new concepts, just applying the real/nominal distinction from §1 and the indexering concepts from §2 to different economic variables. Exercise (e) is a synthesis question that prepares for exam-style evaluative answers. One lesson comfortable.

# RECOMMENDATIONS

## Paragraphs to watch in practice:

1. **Book 1, Ch4§4 (MO=MK rule):** The approximate MK calculation in exercise (e) uses informal differentiation ("each extra unit costs approximately 4Q"). Monitor whether this creates confusion when formal differentiation is introduced in Book 3. If so, keep Book 1's treatment strictly table-based and save all algebraic MO=MK for Book 3.
