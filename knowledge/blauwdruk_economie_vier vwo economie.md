# DESIGN CONSIDERATIONS

This section describes the philosophy and choices behind the course design. Read this before the detailed blueprint — it explains why things are placed where they are.

## 1. Exercise-first design

Every theory paragraph starts with a **target exercise**: the exercise a student should be able to complete at the end of that lesson (1 hour of class + ~30 minutes of homework). This is a deliberate choice. Learning objectives in abstract language ("the student can analyse...") quickly lose precision. A concrete exercise makes unambiguously clear what the student actually needs to *do*: read a graph, perform a calculation, draw a diagram, construct an argument. It also immediately exposes whether a paragraph is too heavy or too light for one lesson.

## 2. Lean paragraphs, rich consolidation

Theory paragraphs are kept lean, focused on one core skill or concept per lesson. They deliberately avoid heavy use of extended text sources, data tables, and multiple exam sources. This is not because those skills are unimportant — they are essential — but because they belong in a different learning phase.

**Consolidation paragraphs** (one per chapter, no new theory) are the place where students encounter exam-style packaging: longer texts, tables, graphs as source material, combined questions requiring integration of multiple skills. This separation ensures that students first master the raw skill cleanly and then practise applying it under realistic exam conditions with more complex framing. Consolidation paragraphs should include contexts drawn from news articles, CBS data, EU policy documents, and other authentic sources.

## 3. Even difficulty distribution

A key design requirement was that **no single paragraph should in practice take more than one lesson**. Teaching experience shows that a lesson that unexpectedly takes two lessons disrupts the entire schedule. Every paragraph has been assessed for realistic lesson time. Where the content was too heavy for one lesson, it has been split across paragraphs or redistributed across chapters. This is why, for example, cost structures are divided over two paragraphs (totals/averages, then marginals), and profit maximisation under monopoly is preceded by a separate lesson on "understanding MR under monopoly."

Book 4 is deliberately lighter than Books 2-3 to allow room for end-of-year fatigue and to serve as a buffer if earlier books overrun.

## 4. Paragraph and chapter structure

Each book has 5 chapters: 4 theory chapters and 1 test preparation chapter. Each theory chapter has 3 theory paragraphs and 1 consolidation paragraph (4 paragraphs total). Exception: Book 1 Chapter 4 has 4 theory paragraphs + consolidation (5 total), because the marginal analysis needed to be split for didactic reasons.

Total number of lessons: ~49 theory lessons + 16 consolidation + 4 test preparation chapters = 73-80 lessons. At 2 lessons per week over ~36 school weeks, this fits.

## 5. Building-block sequencing

The course follows a strict prerequisite ordering. Every paragraph builds on earlier paragraphs, and the blueprint deliberately avoids introducing concepts before the foundation has been laid. Key sequencing chains:

- **Percentages and graphs** (Book 1 Ch1) -> **elasticity calculations** (Book 2 Ch1)
- **Cost structures** (Book 1 Ch3 S2) -> **marginal costs** (Book 1 Ch4 S3) -> **MR=MC rule** (Book 1 Ch4 S4) -> **profit maximisation under perfect competition** (Book 3 Ch1 S2) -> **profit maximisation under monopoly** (Book 3 Ch2 S3)
- **Surplus analysis** (Book 2 Ch2) -> **welfare effects of taxes** (Book 2 Ch3) -> **welfare effects of monopoly** (Book 3 Ch3 S1) -> **welfare effects of minimum wage on the labour market** (Book 4 Ch2 S2) -> **welfare effects of import tariffs** (Book 4 Ch3 S3)

These chains mean that weakness in early skills cascades forward. The consolidation paragraphs serve as checkpoints to detect gaps before they become problematic.

## 6. Differentiation comes before mathematics

Students encounter differentiation of simple quadratic functions for the first time in this economics course, typically before their mathematics course covers it. The blueprint addresses this by introducing differentiation as a practical recipe ("power rule: multiply by the exponent, reduce the power by one") applied to economic functions, not as a mathematical concept. The progression is:

1. Marginals from tables and subtraction (Book 1 Ch4 S3)
2. Differentiate TC to find MC (Book 3 Ch1 S2) — first use of the rule
3. Differentiate TR to find MR under monopoly (Book 3 Ch2 S2-S3) — full application

We only need to differentiate quadratic equations. Coordinate with mathematics colleagues when Book 3 Chapter 2 is being taught.

## 7. Context and motivation

Although theory paragraphs are lean, they are not dry. Every target exercise uses a recognisable context (bakeries, cinemas, restaurants, the NS, solar panels). The goal is twofold: maintain student motivation and begin building the transfer skill that exams require. The heavy contextual work — interpreting complex sources, combining multiple concepts in an unfamiliar setting — is concentrated in consolidation paragraphs and test preparation chapters.

## 8. Assessment design

Each book contains two assessment moments. The **small test (45 minutes)** comes halfway through the book, after chapters 1 and 2, to check whether students are on track and catch errors early — before the material builds further. The **large test (120 minutes)** comes at the end of the book, covering all four theory chapters, and is the primary summative assessment. This rhythm means students receive feedback halfway through each book, giving both teacher and students time to adjust before the heavier test. The consolidation paragraphs and test preparation chapters explicitly prepare students for both formats.

# BOOK 1 — Foundations & Supply/Demand

## Chapter 1: Economic thinking & mathematical foundations

### S1 — Scarcity, opportunity cost, economic thinking ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> A farmer has 10 hectares of land. He can grow wheat (profit EUR 500/ha) or corn (profit EUR 350/ha). He decides to grow wheat on all 10 hectares.
> (a) What is his revenue from wheat?
> (b) What are his opportunity costs?
> (c) A neighbour uses 10 hectares for both crops: 6 ha wheat and 4 ha corn. Calculate her total profit. Has she made a better choice? Explain using the concept of scarcity.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define scarcity as the core problem of economics
- Explain that every choice has opportunity costs
- Calculate opportunity costs in simple scenarios
- Recognise trade-offs in everyday decisions

**Difficulty notes:** Intuitive concepts, concrete examples. Students engage well. No computational barriers. One lesson is comfortable with room for class discussion.

---

### S2 — Percentages, percentage changes, index numbers 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> (a) The price of a bicycle rises from EUR 800 to EUR 920. Calculate the percentage change.
> (b) In 2023, a standard grocery basket costs EUR 150. In 2025, the same basket costs EUR 162. The base year is 2023 (index = 100). Calculate the price index number for 2025.
> (c) In 2026, the index number rises to 112. Calculate the price increase from 2025 to 2026 in percentages.
> (d) A student claims: "The index number went from 108 to 112, so inflation is 4%." Explain why this is wrong and calculate the correct figure.

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate percentage changes using (new - old) / old x 100
- Calculate and interpret index numbers
- Distinguish between index point changes and percentage changes
- Apply these skills to economic data (prices, wages, production)

**Difficulty notes:** Students are weaker at percentage calculations than teachers expect. The index-point-vs.-percentage trap (exercise d) is a classic exam mistake. This paragraph lays the foundation for ALL subsequent calculations (elasticities, inflation, real/nominal). Invest time here. One lesson is tight — assign exercises of type (d) as homework.

---

### S3 — Reading and drawing graphs, interpreting tables ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> The table below shows the number of ice creams sold at different prices:
>
> | Price (EUR) | Quantity |
> |-------------|----------|
> | 1.00        | 500      |
> | 1.50        | 400      |
> | 2.00        | 300      |
> | 2.50        | 200      |
> | 3.00        | 100      |
>
> (a) Draw this as a graph with price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal axis.
> (b) Read from your graph: how many ice creams are sold at EUR 1.75?
> (c) A newspaper writes: "Ice cream sales fell by 50%." Looking at the table, between which two prices could this have happened? Explain.

**Learning objectives:**
- Read values from graphs and tables
- Draw a graph from table data with correct axes
- Interpolate between data points
- Critically interpret claims using data

**Difficulty notes:** Skills-oriented, hands-on. Students appreciate the concreteness. The main pitfall is swapping axes (price on the horizontal instead of vertical axis). One lesson comfortable.

---

### S4 — Consolidation ⬜

Extra practice with all three skills: combining percentage calculations with graph reading, interpreting index numbers from a table, drawing graphs from data. First introduction of exam-style elements: a short text with a data table, followed by questions requiring both reading and calculating. No new theory.

## Chapter 2: Demand

### S1 — Individual demand, willingness to pay, demand curve ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> Lisa loves pizza. She is willing to pay EUR 8 for her first pizza, EUR 6 for the second, EUR 4 for the third, and EUR 1.50 for the fourth.
> (a) Draw Lisa's individual demand curve (step function) with price on the vertical axis.
> (b) The market price is EUR 5. How many pizzas does Lisa buy? Explain using the concept of willingness to pay.
> (c) The price drops to EUR 3. How many does she buy now?
> (d) Explain in your own words why the demand curve slopes downward.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define willingness to pay
- Explain the relationship between price and quantity demanded
- Draw an individual demand curve from willingness-to-pay data
- Explain the law of demand

**Difficulty notes:** Highly intuitive. Pizza/real-life examples work well. Step functions are visual and concrete. One lesson comfortable — use the extra time for a good class discussion about what determines willingness to pay.

---

### S2 — Demand factors, shift vs. movement along the curve 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The market for butter is in equilibrium at P = EUR 2 and Q = 1000.
> (a) The price of butter rises to EUR 2.50. Show in a graph what happens to the quantity demanded. Is this a shift of the demand curve or a movement along the curve?
> (b) A health report states that margarine is unhealthy. Show in a graph what happens to the demand for butter. Is this a shift or a movement?
> (c) Average income in the country rises by 5%. Butter is a normal good. Show the effect on the demand curve.
> (d) Name four factors that cause the demand curve to shift and give an example for each.

**Learning objectives:**
- Distinguish between a movement along the demand curve (caused by own price change) and a shift of the demand curve (caused by other factors)
- Name and explain demand factors: income, preferences, price of substitutes/complements, expectations
- Represent shifts graphically (left/right)
- Classify goods as substitutes or complements based on context

**Difficulty notes:** The shift-vs.-movement distinction is the most common error in all of microeconomics. Students confuse these for months. The lesson must hammer this with at least 4-5 examples. One lesson is tight but essential — this concept recurs in every subsequent chapter.

---

### S3 — From individual to market demand 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> There are only two consumers in the market for lemonade: Anna and Ben.
>
> | Price (EUR) | Anna's Qd | Ben's Qd |
> |-------------|-----------|----------|
> | 1           | 5         | 8        |
> | 2           | 3         | 5        |
> | 3           | 1         | 2        |
> | 4           | 0         | 1        |
>
> (a) Calculate the market demand at each price.
> (b) Draw the individual demand curves and the market demand curve in the same graph.
> (c) Anna's demand function is Qd = -2P + 7, Ben's is Qd = -3P + 11. Derive the market demand function algebraically.
> (d) At what price does Anna leave the market entirely? What happens to the market demand curve at that point?

**Learning objectives:**
- Explain that market demand is the horizontal summation of individual demand curves
- Calculate market demand from individual data (table method)
- Derive market demand algebraically (add Q's at the same P)
- Recognise the kink in the market curve when a consumer drops out

**Difficulty notes:** The horizontal summation (add Q's, not P's) is counter-intuitive for some students. The algebraic derivation (exercise c) requires facility with linear equations. One lesson works if the algebra skills from Ch1 are solid. The kink (exercise d) can be mentioned but is not essential.

---

### S4 — Consolidation ⬜

Practice exercises combining demand curves, shifts, and aggregation. Introduction of exam-style source material: a short news article about a market (e.g. "avocado prices rise after poor harvest"), followed by questions requiring students to identify shifts, movements, and demand factors. No new theory.

## Chapter 3: Supply & cost structures

### S1 — Supply curve, supply factors, shifts 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> (a) A solar panel manufacturer faces higher silicon prices. Show in a graph what happens to the supply curve for solar panels. Explain.
> (b) The government introduces a subsidy for the production of solar panels. Show the effect on the supply curve.
> (c) Name three factors that cause the supply curve to shift and give an example for each.
> (d) Draw a supply curve. Mark a movement along the curve and a shift of the curve. Label clearly which is which.

**Learning objectives:**
- Draw and interpret a supply curve (upward slope)
- Name supply factors: input prices, technology, number of suppliers, expectations, government policy
- Distinguish between shifts of and movements along the supply curve
- Represent supply shifts graphically

**Difficulty notes:** Mirror image of Ch2 S2. Students pick this up faster because they already know the demand framework. The shift/movement distinction is reinforced. Exercise (c) deliberately does not give away the answer about own price — if a student names "own price" as a shift factor, this reveals that they do not understand the movement-vs.-shift distinction. This diagnostic value is important. One lesson comfortable.

---

### S2 — Cost structures: TC, VC, FC, ATC, AVC, AFC 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A bakery has fixed costs (constant costs) of EUR 500 per month (rent, insurance). Each loaf costs EUR 0.80 in ingredients and energy (variable costs per unit).
> (a) Write the formulas for TC, TVC, and TFC as a function of Q.
> (b) Calculate TC, TVC, TFC at Q = 500 and Q = 1000.
> (c) Calculate ATC, AVC, and AFC at Q = 500 and Q = 1000.
> (d) What happens to ATC as Q increases? Explain why this makes sense.
> (e) A student says: "AFC is always the same." Is this correct? Explain.

**Learning objectives:**
- Distinguish between fixed/constant costs (FC) and variable costs (VC)
- Calculate TC = TFC + TVC
- Calculate averages: ATC = TC/Q, AVC = TVC/Q, AFC = TFC/Q
- Understand that AFC falls as Q increases (spreading of fixed costs)
- Understand that AVC is constant when VC per unit is constant

**Difficulty notes:** Seven cost concepts is a lot of terminology. The core insight (ATC falls because fixed costs are spread) is intuitive. Exercises (d) and (e) test conceptual understanding, not just calculation. One lesson works because the formulas are all simple divisions — the challenge is keeping the abbreviations straight. Recommendation: a summary card that students keep in their notebook.

---

### S3 — Revenue: TR, AR, profit, break-even ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> The bakery from S2 sells bread for EUR 1.50 per unit.
> (a) Write the formula for TR as a function of Q.
> (b) Calculate TR and profit (TR - TC) at Q = 500 and Q = 1000.
> (c) Calculate AR (= TR/Q) at Q = 500. What do you notice?
> (d) At what output level does the bakery exactly break even (profit = 0)? Show this with a calculation.
> (e) Draw a graph with TC and TR. Mark the break-even point. Shade the profit area at Q = 1000.

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate TR = P x Q
- Calculate AR = TR/Q (and notice that AR = P at constant price)
- Calculate profit = TR - TC
- Find the break-even point algebraically (TR = TC)
- Graph TC and TR and identify profit/loss areas

**Difficulty notes:** Lighter than S2 because there are fewer new concepts. The break-even calculation is simple algebra. The graph (exercise e) builds a bridge to visual analysis. One lesson comfortable — good moment to check whether the cost concepts from S2 have stuck.

---

### S4 — Consolidation 🟨

Integrative exercises combining costs and revenue. Includes: "at which Q is profit maximal?" with tables (NO marginal analysis yet — that comes in Ch4). Includes a short text context (e.g. a bakery entrepreneur's annual report) with a cost/revenue table, where students must extract and calculate. Build intuition for "there is an optimal point."

## Chapter 4: Market equilibrium & marginal analysis

*Note: this chapter has 4 theory paragraphs + consolidation (5 total) because the marginal analysis requires two separate lessons — one to build the concepts, one to apply the profit maximisation rule.*

### S1 — Equilibrium: solving Qs = Qd 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> In the market for notebooks: Qd = -2P + 100 and Qs = 3P - 25.
> (a) Calculate the equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity.
> (b) Verify your answer by substituting back into both equations.
> (c) Draw both curves in a graph and mark the equilibrium point.
> (d) At a price of EUR 30, is there a surplus or a shortage? Calculate the magnitude.

**Learning objectives:**
- Set Qs = Qd and solve for P (equilibrium price)
- Substitute back to find the equilibrium quantity
- Draw supply and demand curves and locate the equilibrium graphically
- Determine a surplus or shortage at a given price

**Difficulty notes:** First real algebraic challenge. Students who struggle with rearranging equations need support. Exercise (b) — verifying the answer — builds good habits. Exercise (d) bridges to government policy (Book 2). One lesson is tight if algebra is weak — give a worked example first, then let students practise.

---

### S2 — Shifts in supply and demand, new equilibria 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Starting from the notebook market (Qd = -2P + 100, Qs = 3P - 25, equilibrium P* = 25, Q* = 50):
> (a) A new factory opens. The new supply function is Qs = 3P - 10. Calculate the new equilibrium price and quantity.
> (b) Compare with the old equilibrium: what has happened to the price? To the quantity? Does this make sense?
> (c) Suppose students' income rises. The new demand function is Qd = -2P + 120 (supply back to original). Calculate the new equilibrium.
> (d) Draw a graph with both changes (one at a time). Use arrows to indicate the direction of the shifts.
> (e) Suppose BOTH changes happen simultaneously: supply shifts to Qs = 3P - 10 AND demand shifts to Qd = -2P + 120. Calculate the new equilibrium. What has happened to the quantity? What has happened to the price? Could you have predicted the direction of the price change without calculating? Explain.

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate a new equilibrium after a supply or demand shift
- Compare old and new equilibrium: predict the direction of price and quantity changes
- Connect shifts to the demand/supply factors from Ch2 and Ch3
- Represent shifts graphically with clear before/after
- Analyse simultaneous shifts: the quantity effect is unambiguous (both increase Q), but the price effect is indeterminate (one pushes up, the other pushes down)

**Difficulty notes:** Exercise (e) is the key diagnostic question. When both curves shift rightward, quantity unambiguously increases, but the price change depends on the relative magnitudes of the shifts — it can rise, fall, or stay the same. Students who understand the model grasp this; students who just plug in numbers are surprised. This is an important conceptual checkpoint. One lesson works — the algebra is repetition from S1, the challenge is the reasoning in (e).

---

### S3 — Marginal concepts: MC and MR 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A firm has TC = 200 + 3Q (costs in EUR, Q in units) and sells at a fixed price of EUR 8 per unit, so TR = 8Q.
> (a) Fill in the table:
>
> | Q   | TC  | TR  | Profit | MC (= delta TC as Q rises by 1) | MR (= delta TR as Q rises by 1) |
> |-----|-----|-----|--------|------|------|
> | 0   |     |     |        |  —   |  —   |
> | 10  |     |     |        |      |      |
> | 20  |     |     |        |      |      |
> | 30  |     |     |        |      |      |
> | 40  |     |     |        |      |      |
> | 50  |     |     |        |      |      |
>
> (b) What do you notice about MC? Is it constant or variable?
> (c) What do you notice about MR? Why does this make sense at a constant price?
> (d) A second firm has TC = 100 + Q squared and sells at EUR 30 per unit. Fill in a similar table for Q = 0, 5, 10, 15, 20. What happens to MC now?
> (e) Explain in your own words: what does MC tell you? What does MR tell you?

**Learning objectives:**
- Define MC as the extra cost of producing one additional unit (delta TC / delta Q)
- Define MR as the extra revenue from selling one additional unit (delta TR / delta Q)
- Calculate MC and MR from a table using subtraction
- Observe that MC is constant with linear TC, but rising with quadratic TC
- Observe that MR equals the price when the price is constant
- Build intuitive understanding of marginal thinking

**Difficulty notes:** This paragraph introduces the concepts and calculation only — NOT yet the profit maximisation rule. Students work with tables and subtraction, discovering patterns. No differentiation yet. The quadratic cost function in exercise (d) prepares students for rising MC, which they will encounter in Book 3. One lesson works well because the focus is on understanding, not on a complex procedure.

---

### S4 — Profit maximisation: the MR = MC rule 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Use Firm 2 from S3 (TC = 100 + Q squared, TR = 30Q):
> (a) Extend your table from S3(d) and add a column for profit at each Q.
> (b) At which Q is profit highest?
> (c) Look at your MC and MR columns. At the profit-maximising Q, what holds for MC and MR?
> (d) Explain the rule: "produce as long as MR >= MC, stop when MR = MC."
> (e) A third firm has TC = 50 + 2Q squared and sells at P = 40. Calculate the profit-maximising Q using the MR = MC rule WITHOUT making a full table. (Hint: MC = delta TC / delta Q. For TC = 50 + 2Q squared, each additional unit costs approximately 4Q. So set 4Q = 40.)
> (f) Verify your answer from (e) by calculating profit at Q-1, Q, and Q+1. Is profit indeed highest at your answer?

**Learning objectives:**
- Discover through the table that profit is maximal where MR = MC
- Formulate the profit maximisation rule: produce where MR = MC
- Understand why: if MR > MC, one more unit adds to profit; if MR < MC, it reduces profit
- Apply the rule directly (without a full table) by setting up MR = MC as an equation
- Verify the solution by checking profit at neighbouring quantities

**Difficulty notes:** Exercise (e) is the bridge to algebraic application. The hint deliberately uses an approximated MC ("each additional unit costs approximately 4Q") rather than formal differentiation — that comes in Book 3. Students learn the logic of the rule here; the calculus technique comes later. Exercise (f) builds verification habits. One lesson works because the rule emerges naturally from the table work in S3.

---

### S5 — Consolidation 🟨

Complete market equilibrium exercises: given functions, find equilibrium, calculate costs/revenue/profit, apply the marginal rule. Includes exam-style context: a short text about a fruit stall at the market with a cost table, where students must find the profit-maximising output and explain their reasoning. Combine graph reading with calculations.

## Chapter 5: Test preparation

Practice test covering all content from Chapters 1-4. This prepares students for the 120-minute large test at the end of the book. Includes graph drawing, calculations, and short explanation questions. Use longer exam-style contexts with data tables and graphs as sources. Note: the 45-minute small test comes after Chapter 2 and covers only Chapters 1-2.

# BOOK 2 — Elasticities, surplus & welfare

## Chapter 1: Elasticities

### S1 — Price elasticity: concept and calculation 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A cinema raises its ticket price from EUR 10.00 to EUR 12.00. The number of visitors falls from 500 to 420 per week.
> (a) Calculate the percentage change in price.
> (b) Calculate the percentage change in quantity demanded.
> (c) Calculate the price elasticity of demand (Ed = %delta Qd / %delta P).
> (d) Is the demand for cinema tickets elastic or inelastic? Explain what this means in plain language.
> (e) A petrol station raises its price from EUR 1.80 to EUR 1.90. Sales fall from 10,000 to 9,800 litres. Calculate Ed. Is petrol elastic or inelastic? Why does this make sense?

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate percentage changes for price and quantity
- Apply the formula Ed = %delta Qd / %delta P
- Classify demand as elastic (|Ed| > 1) or inelastic (|Ed| < 1)
- Intuitively explain why some goods are more elastic than others (substitutes, necessity vs. luxury)

**Difficulty notes:** The formula itself is simple, but students make errors in the percentage calculation (using new instead of old as the base, forgetting the sign). Exercise (e) provides immediate contrast — elastic vs. inelastic — which reinforces understanding. One lesson is feasible if the percentage skills from Book 1 Ch1 S2 are solid. If not, this overruns.

---

### S2 — Elastic vs. inelastic: the link to revenue 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the cinema from S1 (price up from EUR 10 to EUR 12, visitors fall from 500 to 420):
> (a) Calculate revenue (total revenue = P x Q) before and after the price increase.
> (b) Has revenue risen or fallen? Explain this using the elasticity you calculated.
> (c) The cinema owner wants to maximise revenue. Based on the Ed, should he raise or lower the price further? Explain the rule.
> (d) Now consider the petrol station (price up from EUR 1.80 to EUR 1.90, litres from 10,000 to 9,800). Calculate revenue before and after. Did the price increase help the petrol station's revenue? Why?
> (e) Complete the rule: "If demand is elastic, a price increase leads to ... revenue. If demand is inelastic, a price increase leads to ... revenue."

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate revenue = P x Q before and after a price change
- Explain that elastic demand means quantity falls proportionally more than price rises -> revenue falls
- Explain that inelastic demand means quantity falls proportionally less -> revenue rises
- Apply the rule to advise a business on pricing strategy

**Difficulty notes:** This is where elasticity becomes meaningful — students see why firms care about it. Note that the formula revenue = P x Q (total revenue) is used formally for the first time this year here. Students have an intuitive idea of revenue, but this is the first time they apply the formula systematically and link it to a concept (elasticity). The rule "elastic -> price increase means revenue decrease" is counter-intuitive for some. One lesson works — the calculations are simple repetitions from S1, the new element is the interpretation.

---

### S3 — Income elasticity and cross-price elasticity 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> *Part A — Income elasticity:*
> (a) Average household income rises by 5%. Demand for organic food rises by 8%. Demand for budget noodles falls by 3%.
> Calculate the income elasticity (Ei) for organic food and for noodles.
> Classify each: normal good or inferior good? Luxury or necessity?
>
> *Part B — Cross-price elasticity:*
> (b) The price of coffee rises by 10%. Demand for tea rises by 4%. Demand for coffee filters falls by 6%.
> Calculate the cross-price elasticity (Ec) for tea with respect to the coffee price, and for coffee filters with respect to the coffee price.
> Classify: are tea and coffee substitutes or complements? And coffee and coffee filters?
>
> *Part C — Demand functions with multiple variables:*
> (c) Demand for good X is given by: Qx = 100 - 2Px + 0.5Py + 0.01Y, where Px is the price of X, Py is the price of good Y, and Y is income.
> Currently: Px = 10, Py = 20, Y = 30,000.
> Calculate the current quantity demanded of X.
> Income rises to Y = 33,000 (other factors unchanged). Calculate the new Qd. Calculate Ei.
> The price of Y rises from 20 to 24 (other factors at original values). Calculate the new Qd. Calculate Ec. Are X and Y substitutes or complements?
>
> (d) Using the demand function from (c): predict what happens to demand for X if a recession reduces income by 10%. Calculate the new Qd.

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate Ei = %delta Qd / %delta Income
- Classify goods: normal (Ei > 0) vs. inferior (Ei < 0), necessity (0 < Ei < 1) vs. luxury (Ei > 1)
- Calculate Ec = %delta Qd(good A) / %delta P(good B)
- Classify good pairs: substitutes (Ec > 0) vs. complements (Ec < 0)
- Read and use a demand function with multiple variables (price of other goods, income)
- Use elasticity for predictions

**Difficulty notes:** Parts A and B use the same formula structure as Ed — same skill, different variables. Students who have mastered S1 pick this up quickly. Part C introduces demand functions with multiple variables, which appear in exam questions. Students need to be comfortable substituting multiple values and holding other variables constant (ceteris paribus in action). This is the most novel element. One lesson works because parts A and B go quickly; part C takes more time but is essential for exam preparation.

---

### S4 — Consolidation 🟨

Mixed elasticity problems with exam-style source material. Includes a table with real market data (e.g. CBS data on consumer spending), where students must calculate the relevant elasticities. Includes: "given this Ed, what happens to revenue if the price changes by X%?" Includes a problem with a multi-variable demand function in a news context. Includes a question where students must determine from the context which elasticity needs to be calculated.

## Chapter 2: Consumer surplus, producer surplus & Pareto efficiency

### S1 — Consumer surplus 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The demand curve for concert tickets is P = 50 - 0.5Q (P in EUR, Q in tickets).
> The market price is EUR 20.
> (a) Calculate the quantity demanded at this price.
> (b) Draw the demand curve. Shade the area representing consumer surplus.
> (c) Calculate the consumer surplus. (Hint: it is a triangle.)
> (d) Explain in your own words what consumer surplus means. Why is it a measure of buyer welfare?

**Learning objectives:**
- Define consumer surplus as the difference between willingness to pay and actual price, summed over all buyers
- Graphically identify CS as the triangle between the demand curve and the price line
- Calculate CS as the area of a triangle: 1/2 x base x height
- Interpret CS as a measure of buyer welfare

**Difficulty notes:** The concept is visual and concrete. The calculation (area of a triangle) is simple geometry. The main stumbling block is correctly identifying which triangle — students sometimes shade the wrong area. One lesson comfortable.

### S2 — Producer surplus, total surplus, and the link to marginal cost 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The supply curve for concert tickets is P = 5 + 0.25Q. The demand curve is P = 50 - 0.5Q.
> (a) Calculate the equilibrium price and quantity.
> (b) Draw both curves. Shade and label CS and PS in different colours.
> (c) Calculate CS and PS separately.
> (d) Calculate total surplus (TS = CS + PS).
> (e) The supply curve represents the marginal cost of each additional ticket. Explain why producer surplus can also be read as: "the difference between the price received and the marginal cost of production, summed over all units sold." Show this in your graph.
> (f) A promoter asks: "Is this the best possible outcome for society?" Answer using the concept of total surplus.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define producer surplus as the difference between the price actually received and the minimum acceptable price, summed over all sellers
- Graphically identify PS (triangle between price line and supply curve)
- Calculate PS as the area of a triangle
- Calculate total surplus = CS + PS
- Understand that the supply curve IS the marginal cost curve — so PS is the area between price and MC
- Begin to understand that the competitive equilibrium maximises total surplus

**Difficulty notes:** The graphical work for PS mirrors CS — students pick it up quickly. The challenging element is exercise (e): connecting PS to marginal cost. The supply curve has so far been treated as "willingness to sell"; now students must see it as the MC curve. This dual interpretation (supply = MC) is crucial for understanding welfare effects of monopoly in Book 3. Spend time on this link — it is more important than the triangle calculation itself. One lesson works if you prioritise the MC link over drilling calculations.

### S3 — Pareto efficiency and introduction of deadweight loss 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the market from S2 (equilibrium P = EUR 20, Q = 60, TS = 1350):
> (a) Suppose the government forces the price to EUR 30. Calculate the new Qd and Qs. Which determines the actual number of transactions? Why?
> (b) Calculate CS and PS at the forced price of EUR 30.
> (c) Calculate the new total surplus. How much surplus has been lost compared to the free-market equilibrium?
> (d) The lost surplus is called deadweight loss. Shade it in your graph.
> (e) Explain in your own words what Pareto-efficient means. Why is the free-market equilibrium Pareto-efficient and the situation at P = EUR 30 not?

**Learning objectives:**
- Define Pareto efficiency: no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off
- Understand that the competitive equilibrium is Pareto-efficient (TS is maximal)
- Calculate deadweight loss when the price deviates from equilibrium
- Recognise that any price above or below equilibrium creates deadweight loss
- Graphically identify deadweight loss (the "lost triangle")

**Difficulty notes:** The Pareto concept is abstract, but exercise (e) makes it concrete: "can you improve someone's position without harming another?" The deadweight loss calculation is straightforward (it is a triangle again). The conceptual challenge is that students need to see why transactions are "lost." One lesson works because the mathematics is repetition — the new element is the interpretation.

## Chapter 3: Government intervention — taxes & subsidies

### S1 — Taxes: the wedge and the new equilibrium 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**

**Learning objectives:**

- Calculate the new equilibrium with tax
- Distinguish between consumer price and producer price
- Represent the tax wedge graphically

**Difficulty notes:** The concept "the supply curve shifts up by the tax amount" is often taught but rarely understood. Students confuse "upward shift = less supply." ### S2 — Taxes: incidence, pass-through, and deadweight loss 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the market from S1 (original: P* = 7, Q* = 6; after EUR 3 tax: Pc = 8, Pp = 5, Qt = 3):
> (a) How much of the EUR 3 tax is paid by the consumer? How much by the producer?

> (c) Calculate CS before and after the tax.
> (d) Calculate PS before and after the tax.
> (e) Calculate total tax revenue for the government.

- Calculate CS, PS, and tax revenue in the post-tax situation
- Calculate deadweight loss from taxation

**Difficulty notes:** This is a calculation-intensive lesson with 5-6 separate computations. ## Chapter 1: Perfect competition

### S1 — Characteristics of perfect competition ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> A wheat farmer sells wheat at the market price of EUR 200 per ton. He cannot influence this price.
> (a) Name four characteristics of a perfectly competitive market. Explain for each why the wheat market meets this condition.
> (b) The farmer considers charging EUR 210 per ton. Explain why this does not work.
> (c) Draw the farmer's individual price line. Label it P = AR = MR.
> (d) Explain why MR = price for this farmer.

**Learning objectives:**
- Name the four characteristics: many buyers and sellers, homogeneous product, free entry and exit, perfect information
- Explain why individual firms are quantity adjusters (price takers)
- Understand that P = AR = MR under perfect competition (horizontal price line)
- Draw the individual price/revenue curves

**Difficulty notes:** Conceptual lesson. The equality P = AR = MR is stated and visually understood, not mathematically derived. Students find this intuitive ("if you always get the same price, each additional product earns that price"). One lesson comfortable — use the extra time for real-world examples (commodity markets, agriculture).

---

### S2 — Profit maximisation: MR = MC under perfect competition 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The wheat farmer's costs are TC = 0.5Q squared + 10Q + 500 (Q in tons). The market price is EUR 200/ton.
> (a) Write the MC function. (Hint: differentiate TC. Remember: if TC = aQ squared + bQ + c, then MC = 2aQ + b.)
> (b) Write MR. (What is MR for a price taker?)
> (c) Set MR = MC and solve for Q. This is the profit-maximising quantity.
> (d) Calculate total revenue, total cost, and total profit at this Q.
> (e) Draw a graph with MC and the horizontal price/MR line. Mark the profit-maximising point.
> (f) Would this farmer produce at Q = 100? Explain using MR and MC.

**Learning objectives:**
- Differentiate a simple quadratic TC function to find MC (power rule: multiply by the exponent, reduce the power by one)
- Establish MR = P for a price-taking firm
- Find the profit-maximising output by setting MR = MC
- Calculate profit at the optimal Q
- Represent the MR = MC solution graphically
- Explain why producing beyond MR = MC reduces profit

**Difficulty notes:** This is where differentiation enters the course. The power rule on a quadratic is simple ("multiply by 2, reduce the power of Q by 1"). Students must see this as a recipe. The conceptual link ("marginal cost tells you what one extra ton costs") was laid in Book 1 Ch4 S3-S4. Exercise (f) tests whether they understand the rule, not just apply it. One lesson is tight — spend the first 15 minutes on the differentiation technique, then apply.

### S3 — Long-run equilibrium: entry and exit 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> The wheat farmer from S2 is making a profit.
> (a) Explain what will happen in the long run: will new farmers enter or exit? What happens to total market supply?
> (b) In a market diagram (supply and demand), show what happens to the market price as supply increases.
> (c) At what profit level does entry stop? Explain.
> (d) In the long-run equilibrium, price equals the minimum of ATC. Using TC = 0.5Q squared + 10Q + 500, write the formula for ATC. Set ATC = MC (= Q + 10) and solve for Q. This is the efficient scale of the firm.
> (e) Calculate ATC at the Q you found in (d). This is the long-run equilibrium price. Verify that profit = 0 at this price.
> (f) Draw the long-run equilibrium for the individual firm: P = ATC at the minimum of ATC, with the MC curve through that point. Explain why this is the long-run outcome.

**Learning objectives:**
- Explain that positive profit attracts entry; negative profit leads to exit
- Understand that entry shifts market supply rightward -> price falls
- Understand that in long-run equilibrium: economic profit = 0
- Calculate the efficient scale by setting ATC = MC and solving for Q
- Calculate the long-run equilibrium price from the minimum of ATC
- Show that the long-run equilibrium occurs where P = minimum ATC = MC
- Draw the long-run equilibrium for the individual firm

**Difficulty notes:** Conceptual reasoning about dynamics plus a meaningful calculation. Exercise (d) is the core skill: setting ATC = MC. Students write ATC = TC/Q = 0.5Q + 10 + 500/Q, set this equal to MC = Q + 10, giving 0.5Q + 10 + 500/Q = Q + 10, simplified to 500/Q = 0.5Q, so Q squared = 1000, Q approximately 31.6. This algebra is demanding — the 500/Q term is unfamiliar territory. Guide students through this step by step. Exercise (e) gives the satisfying verification (profit = 0). One lesson works if you prioritise the calculation and keep the graphical work in (f) brief.

### S2 — MR under monopoly: understanding the downward-sloping MR curve 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A monopolist has demand Qd = 100 - 2P, i.e. P = 50 - 0.5Q.
> (a) Fill in the table:
>
> | Q  | P    | TR (=P x Q) | MR (=delta TR / delta Q) |
> |----|------|-------------|--------------------------|
> | 0  |      |             |                          |
> | 10 |      |             |                          |
> | 20 |      |             |                          |
> | 30 |      |             |                          |
> | 40 |      |             |                          |
> | 50 |      |             |                          |
> | 60 |      |             |                          |
>
> (b) At which Q is TR maximal? What is MR at that point?
> (c) Draw the demand curve (P-curve) and the MR curve in the same graph. What do you notice about the slope of MR compared to the demand curve?
> (d) Explain in words why MR is lower than the price for a monopolist. (Hint: to sell one more unit, the monopolist must lower the price on ALL units.)

**Learning objectives:**

**Difficulty notes:** This is the essential preparation lesson for S3. The table method (exercises a-c) builds intuition BEFORE the algebra (exercise e). ### S3 — Profit maximisation under monopoly: complete calculation 🟥->🟨 HEAVY (made manageable)

**Target exercise:**
> The monopolist from S2 has demand P = 50 - 0.5Q and costs TC = 0.25Q squared + 5Q + 100.
> Step by step:
> (a) Write TR as a function of Q: TR = (50 - 0.5Q) x Q. Expand.
> (b) Differentiate TR to find MR.
> (c) Differentiate TC to find MC.
> (d) Set MR = MC. Solve for Q.
> (e) Find the price by substituting Q back into the demand function P = 50 - 0.5Q.
> (f) Calculate total profit: Profit = TR - TC.
> (g) Draw the graph: demand curve, MR, MC. Mark the profit-maximising Q and P. Shade the profit area.
> (h) Could the monopolist charge a higher price than your answer in (e)? What would happen to quantity and profit?

**Learning objectives:**
- Execute the complete monopoly profit maximisation procedure:
  1. Derive TR from the demand function
  2. Differentiate TR -> MR
  3. Differentiate TC -> MC
  4. Solve MR = MC -> optimal Q
  5. Substitute back into demand -> optimal P
  6. Calculate profit
- Draw the complete monopoly graph with MR, MC, demand curve
- Graphically identify the profit area

**Difficulty notes:** The most demanding lesson of the entire course. 6 algebraic steps plus a graph. But students are prepared: MC differentiation (Ch1 S2), MR table and differentiation (Ch2 S2), MR = MC rule (Book 1 Ch4 S3-S4). The exercise is scaffolded step by step. ## Chapter 4: Market failure & externalities

### S1 — Negative externalities 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A factory produces chemicals. The private marginal cost is MPC = 2Q. But production causes water pollution that costs nearby fishermen EUR 4 per unit. The social marginal cost is therefore MSC = 2Q + 4. Market demand is P = 20 - Q.
> (a) Calculate the market equilibrium (where demand = MPC). This is what the market produces without government intervention.
> (b) Calculate the socially optimal equilibrium (where demand = MSC).
> (c) Draw both equilibria in one graph. Shade the deadweight loss from overproduction.
> (d) How many units too many does the free market produce?
> (e) Explain why this is called market failure. Who bears the costs that the market ignores?

**Learning objectives:**
- Define externalities: costs/benefits that affect third parties not involved in the transaction
- Distinguish between private costs and social costs: MSC = MPC + external costs
- Calculate market equilibrium (using MPC) and socially optimal equilibrium (using MSC)
- Calculate the deadweight loss from overproduction (the market produces too much)
- Explain why externalities cause market failure: the market price does not reflect the full costs

**Difficulty notes:** New graph type: two supply curves (MPC and MSC). Students must understand why the market uses MPC (because firms do not pay for pollution) while society should use MSC. The mathematics is simple — it is the same equilibrium calculation as in Book 1, just performed twice. One lesson works.

---

### S2 — Positive externalities 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Vaccinations have positive externalities: each vaccinated person protects others (herd immunity). Private marginal benefit is MPB. Social marginal benefit is MSB = MPB + EUR 5 (the benefit to others).
> Demand (= MPB): P = 30 - Q. Supply (= MC): P = Q.
> (a) Calculate the market equilibrium (where supply = MPB).
> (b) Calculate the socially optimal equilibrium (where supply = MSB). For this: MSB = 30 - Q + 5 = 35 - Q. Set equal to supply.
> (c) The market produces too little. How many units too few?
> (d) Draw both equilibria. Shade the deadweight loss from underproduction.
> (e) Propose a policy measure to correct this market failure. How large must the correction be?

**Learning objectives:**
- Distinguish between private benefits and social benefits: MSB = MPB + external benefit
- Calculate market equilibrium (using MPB) and socially optimal equilibrium (using MSB)
- Understand that the market produces too little of goods with positive externalities
- Propose a correction: subsidy equal to the external benefit per unit
- Draw the graph with MPB and MSB

**Difficulty notes:** Mirror image of S1 — if students have understood negative externalities, this is the same logic in reverse. The policy proposal (exercise e) connects back to Book 2 Ch3 (subsidies). One lesson comfortable.

# BOOK 4 — Labour markets, trade & macro foundations

## Chapter 1: Labour market foundations

### S1 — Labour demand: derived demand and marginal productivity ⬜ LIGHT

**Target exercise:**
> A restaurant hires waiters. Each waiter can serve a certain number of extra meals per evening:
>
> | Waiter # | Extra meals (marginal product) | Revenue per meal |
> |----------|-------------------------------|------------------|
> | 1st      | 50                            | EUR 15           |
> | 2nd      | 45                            | EUR 15           |
> | 3rd      | 35                            | EUR 15           |
> | 4th      | 25                            | EUR 15           |
> | 5th      | 15                            | EUR 15           |
>
> (a) Calculate the value of the marginal product (VMP = marginal product x revenue per meal) for each waiter.
> (b) The wage is EUR 450 per evening. How many waiters should the restaurant hire? Explain using the VMP.
> (c) The wage rises to EUR 600. How many waiters now? What has happened to labour demand?
> (d) Explain why the demand for labour is called "derived demand."
> (e) Draw the restaurant's labour demand curve using the VMP values.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define the marginal product of labour (additional output from one extra worker)
- Calculate the value of the marginal product: VMP = MP x price of output
- Apply the hiring rule: hire as long as VMP >= wage
- Understand derived demand: labour is demanded because of demand for the product
- Draw a labour demand curve (which is the VMP curve)

**Difficulty notes:** Connects directly to the marginal analysis from Book 1 and Book 3 (MR = MC is analogous to VMP = W). Students find the restaurant context appealing. Diminishing marginal productivity is intuitive ("the kitchen gets too crowded"). One lesson comfortable.

### S2 — Labour supply and participation rate 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**

> - Total population aged 15-75: 12 million
> - Labour force: 9 million
> - Unemployed labour force (unemployed, seeking work): 540,000
>

> (b) Calculate the unemployment rate.
> (c) A student says: "3 million people are unemployed because 12 - 9 = 3 million." Explain why this is wrong.

> (e) Draw a labour supply curve. Explain why it has an upward slope.

**Learning objectives:**

- Explain what determines labour supply: wages, preferences, demographics

**Difficulty notes:** The terminology (labour force vs. non-labour force) is the main challenge — students confuse "not working" with "unemployed." Exercise (c) addresses this confusion directly. The formulas are simple. One lesson works with clear emphasis on definitions.

## Chapter 2: Wages, minimum wage & unemployment

### S1 — Types of unemployment 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Classify each situation as cyclical unemployment, structural unemployment, or frictional unemployment. Explain your choice.
> (a) A coal miner in Limburg loses his job because the mine permanently closes due to the transition to renewable energy.
> (b) A marketing manager quits to search for a better position. She is unemployed for 3 months.
> (c) A construction worker is laid off because the housing market collapses during a recession.
> (d) Factory workers are replaced by robots. Their skills do not match available jobs.
> (e) For each type, explain whether it is temporary or can persist for a long time.
> (f) A government wants to reduce structural unemployment. Name two policy measures and explain how they work.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define three types of unemployment: cyclical, structural, frictional
- Classify real-world cases into the correct type
- Understand causes: demand shortfall (cyclical), skills mismatch or automation (structural), search time (frictional)
- Propose policy responses for each type
- Understand that frictional unemployment is normal and even healthy

**Difficulty notes:** Definition-based lesson. The classification can be ambiguous (is the miner structural or frictional?) — this yields good discussion. Exercise (f) requires policy thinking. One lesson works.

### S2 — Minimum wage: welfare effects on the labour market 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using the IT labour market from Ch1 S3: Qd = -200W + 5000, Qs = 300W - 1000 (equilibrium W* = EUR 12, Q* = 2600).
> (a) The government sets a minimum wage of EUR 15/hour. Calculate Qd and Qs at this wage.
> (b) How many workers become unemployed? (= Qs - Qd)
> (c) Draw the labour market with the minimum wage. Shade worker surplus and employer surplus.
> (d) Calculate total surplus before and after the minimum wage.
> (e) Calculate the deadweight loss.
> (f) Some workers earn more (EUR 15 instead of EUR 12), but others lose their jobs. Evaluate: "A minimum wage helps workers." Use your calculations to argue both sides.

**Learning objectives:**
- Apply the price floor framework from Book 2 Ch4 S2 to the labour market
- Calculate unemployment caused by the minimum wage (labour surplus)
- Calculate welfare effects: worker surplus, employer surplus, deadweight loss
- Recognise the trade-off: higher wage for those employed vs. job loss for others
- Formulate a balanced evaluation of minimum wage policy

**Difficulty notes:** This is a direct transfer from Book 2 (minimum price). Students must recognise the framework: a minimum wage IS a price floor on labour. The welfare calculation is identical. The new element is exercise (f) — a balanced evaluation — which is an exam skill (position-taking). One lesson works. If there is a long time gap between Book 2 and this lesson, start with a 10-minute review of price floor mechanics.

## Chapter 3: International trade

### S1 — Absolute and comparative advantage 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Two countries produce cars and grain:
>
> | Country | Hours per car | Hours per ton of grain |
> |---------|---------------|------------------------|
> | A       | 100           | 10                     |
> | B       | 150           | 30                     |
>
> (a) Which country has an absolute advantage in cars? In grain?
> (b) Calculate the opportunity cost of 1 car in each country (in tons of grain forgone).
> (c) Calculate the opportunity cost of 1 ton of grain in each country (in cars forgone).
> (d) Which country has a comparative advantage in cars? In grain? Explain.
> (e) A student says: "Country A is better at everything, so trade is pointless." Explain why this is wrong using your answers.

**Learning objectives:**
- Define absolute advantage (producing more with the same resources)
- Define comparative advantage (lowest opportunity cost)
- Calculate opportunity costs from a production table
- Explain that comparative advantage, not absolute advantage, determines the trade pattern
- Understand that trade benefits both countries, even if one country is better at everything

**Difficulty notes:** The opportunity cost calculation is the tricky part — students must think in terms of "what do you give up." The counter-intuitive result (B can have a comparative advantage despite being worse at everything) is why the concept is difficult. One lesson is tight — reserve time for the "aha moment."

### S2 — Gains from trade, terms of trade 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> Using countries A and B from S1 (A: comparative advantage in grain, B: comparative advantage in cars):
> Before trade, each country uses a total of 3,000 labour hours and splits them equally between both products.
> (a) Calculate the production of each good in each country before trade.
> (b) Now each country fully specialises. Calculate total world production of each good.
> (c) Show that world production of at least one good has risen (without the other falling).
> (d) Country A exports grain to B and imports cars. The terms of trade are: 1 car = 8 tons of grain. After trade, A consumes 15 cars and 185 tons of grain. Is A better off compared to the no-trade situation? Show with numbers.
> (e) At what terms of trade would A refuse to trade? At what terms would B refuse? (Hint: use the opportunity costs from S1.)

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate production before and after specialisation
- Show that specialisation increases total world production
- Define the terms of trade: the exchange ratio between goods
- Determine the range of mutually beneficial terms of trade (between the two opportunity costs)
- Understand that both countries can benefit from trade

**Difficulty notes:** Lots of computation but conceptually straightforward once comparative advantage is understood. Exercise (e) requires linking back to opportunity costs — elegant but potentially confusing. One lesson works if S1 was solid. If not, this overruns.

## Chapter 4: Inflation, purchasing power & prices

### S1 — Inflation, CPI, and price index numbers 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A grocery basket costs EUR 150 in 2023 (base year, index = 100) and EUR 162 in 2025.
> (a) Calculate the CPI for 2025.
> (b) Calculate the inflation rate from 2023 to 2025.
> (c) In 2026, the CPI is 112. Calculate the inflation from 2025 to 2026. (Note: not 4%!)
> (d) A worker's nominal wage rises from EUR 3,000/month in 2023 to EUR 3,200 in 2025. Calculate the real wage change. Has the worker's purchasing power risen or fallen?
> (e) Explain the difference between nominal and real values. Why do economists prefer real values?

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate CPI = (basket cost in year t / basket cost in base year) x 100
- Calculate the inflation rate from CPI values
- Distinguish between index point changes and percentage changes (crucial exam skill!)
- Calculate real values: real wage = nominal wage / CPI x 100
- Explain why real values adjust for inflation

**Difficulty notes:** Exercise (c) is the classic trap — students think "108 to 112 = 4% inflation" when in reality it is (112-108)/108 = 3.7%. This connects directly back to Book 1 Ch1 S2 (percentage changes). The real-vs.-nominal distinction is conceptually important but computationally simple. One lesson works.

---

### S2 — Purchasing power and indexation 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**
> A pension is EUR 1,500 per month. Inflation is 4%. Average wages have risen by 6%.
> (a) The pension is value-protected ("waardevast", indexed to inflation). Calculate the new pension.
> (b) The pension is welfare-protected ("welvaartsvast", indexed to wage growth). Calculate the new pension.
> (c) The pension is frozen. Calculate the real purchasing power loss in euros per month.
> (d) Which indexation method is best for the pensioner? Which is cheapest for the pension fund? Explain the trade-off.
> (e) A country experiences 2% deflation. What happens to the purchasing power of a frozen pension? Is this good or bad for the pensioner?

**Learning objectives:**
- Define "waardevast" (indexed to CPI/inflation) vs. "welvaartsvast" (indexed to wages)
- Calculate pensions/wages after indexation
- Calculate purchasing power changes with frozen pensions
- Understand the trade-off between protecting pensioners and pension fund sustainability
- Apply concepts to deflation (reverse situation)

**Difficulty notes:** The terminology ("waardevast", "welvaartsvast", frozen) is confusing because the words look similar. Exercises (a-c) force precise calculation for each case. Exercise (e) tests deeper understanding. One lesson works — the maths is arithmetic, the challenge is keeping the definitions straight.

### S3 — Real vs. nominal: integration and application 🟨 MEDIUM

**Target exercise:**

> (b) Country X's nominal GDP grew from EUR 800 billion to EUR 840 billion. The GDP deflator rose from 100 to 103. Calculate real GDP growth. Did the country produce more goods and services, or have prices merely risen?
> (c) A worker's nominal wage rises from EUR 2,500 to EUR 2,600 (an increase of 4%). Inflation is 5%. Calculate the real wage change. Is this worker better or worse off?

> (e) Combine your knowledge: explain why a central bank targets low inflation (around 2%). Use the concepts of real interest rate, purchasing power, and workers in your answer.

**Learning objectives:**
- Calculate real interest rate approximately equals nominal interest rate - inflation

**Difficulty notes:** This is an integration lesson — no new concepts, just application of the real/nominal distinction from S1 and the indexation concepts from S2 to various economic variables. # RECOMMENDATIONS

## Paragraphs to monitor in practice:

1. **Book 1, Ch4 S4 (MR=MC rule):** The approximated MC calculation in exercise (e) uses informal differentiation ("each additional unit costs approximately 4Q"). Monitor whether this causes confusion when formal differentiation is introduced in Book 3. If so, keep Book 1's treatment strictly table-based and save all algebraic MR=MC for Book 3.

2. **Book 2, Ch1 S3 (Ei, Ec + demand functions):** Part C (demand function with multiple variables) is new in this version. If it makes the lesson too heavy, move Part C to the consolidation paragraph and keep S3 to just Ei and Ec with simple percentage data.

3. **Book 2, Ch3 S2 (taxes — incidence + deadweight loss):** Five separate calculations in one lesson. Mitigation: do CS and PS together in class; assign the deadweight loss calculation as homework. Alternative: provide a pre-drawn graph so students focus on calculating, not drawing.

4. **Book 3, Ch2 S3 (monopoly profit maximisation):** The hardest individual lesson. Mitigation: the step-by-step scaffolding reduces the risk. Do steps (a-d) as "begeleide inoefening", let students do (e-f) independently.

## Cross-book dependencies to monitor:

- **Book 2 Ch2 (surplus)** depends on Book 1 Ch4 S1 (solving equilibrium). If algebra is weak, surplus calculations will stall.
- **Book 3 Ch2 S2-S3 (monopoly)** depends on differentiation, introduced in Book 3 Ch1 S2. If students need more practice with differentiation, delay monopoly by one lesson and add a pure differentiation practice lesson.
- **Book 4 Ch2 S2 (minimum wage surplus)** depends on Book 2 Ch4 S2 (minimum price). If there is a long time gap, build in 10 minutes of review before this lesson.

## Using Book 4 as a buffer:

Book 4 is deliberately lighter. If Books 2-3 overrun by 2-3 lessons in total, shorten the consolidation lessons in Book 4 or integrate Book 4's test preparation into the final test preparation chapter rather than doing it separately. The consolidation paragraphs in Book 4 (all rated ⬜) can absorb compression without losing essential content.
