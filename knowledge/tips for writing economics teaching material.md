# How to write an excellent economics textbook for ages 15–18

**The most effective economics textbooks share a common DNA: they start with real-world problems, build concepts through carefully scaffolded worked examples, integrate graphs directly with explanatory text, and interleave practice across chapters.** This matters because the gap between research-validated textbook design and actual publishing practice remains wide—studies show standard instruction barely dents student misconceptions, while evidence-based approaches like interleaving boost retention by 30% or more. The research base draws from cognitive load theory, multimedia learning science, economics education journals, and practitioner experience across AP, A-level, IB, and Dutch vwo/havo systems. What follows is a synthesis of that evidence, organized as practical guidance for authors.

---

## Start micro, scaffold relentlessly, and spiral back

The sequencing debate—microeconomics first or macroeconomics first—has generated conflicting research, but the weight of evidence favors **micro before macro**. Fizel and Johnson (1986) found micro-first students performed 10–50% better on standardized economics tests, and Terry and Galchus (2003), studying 870 students, confirmed micro-before-macro as the second-best sequence (after taking both concurrently). The logic is pedagogically intuitive: micro concepts like individual choice, supply and demand in a single market, and price mechanisms connect to students' daily experience—buying goods, choosing how to spend time. Macro aggregates are more abstract and build naturally on micro foundations. The AP Economics framework, the Council for Economic Education standards, and most top-ranked economics departments follow this convention.

Within this sequence, scaffolding must be systematic and deliberate. Claar and Finn (2011) identified a critical problem: "Students often become frustrated with learning economics not because the economics is too hard, but because they try to grasp the economic device, model, or tool being used at the same time they try to understand the economic theory the device is designed to illuminate." Their solution combines three techniques: activating prior knowledge by connecting new concepts to what students already know, providing structured temporary support that fades over time, and modeling how to use economic tools before requiring independent use. Each concept should follow a five-layer progression: concrete familiar example → formal model with worked example → guided practice with partial scaffolding → independent practice → real-world application.

Bruner's spiral curriculum provides the macro-architecture. Core concepts like opportunity cost, supply and demand, and market equilibrium should reappear at increasing complexity. Opportunity cost, for instance, might first appear as personal spending choices, return as the production possibilities frontier for firms, reappear as government budget trade-offs, and culminate in cost-benefit analysis of regulation. But poorly implemented spiraling becomes what Schmidt, McKnight, and Raizen (1997) call "a mile wide and an inch deep"—mere repetition without genuine deepening. The countermeasure is to **advance through Bloom's taxonomy with each revisit**: remember on first encounter, understand on second, apply on third, analyze and evaluate on subsequent passes. Hattie's meta-analysis places the effect size of spaced practice—a core spiral mechanism—at **0.71**, making it one of the most impactful teaching strategies available.

## Cognitive load theory dictates the fine-grained design decisions

Sweller's Cognitive Load Theory is, as Dylan Wiliam put it in 2017, "the single most important thing for teachers to know," and its implications for textbook design are specific and actionable. Working memory holds approximately **four chunks** of information simultaneously (Cowan, 2001). Every design decision must ask: does this reduce extraneous load, manage intrinsic load, and maximize germane (learning-productive) load?

The **split-attention effect** is perhaps the most violated principle in economics publishing. When students must process a supply-demand diagram on one part of the page and its explanation in a separate paragraph, they waste cognitive resources integrating the two sources. The evidence is unequivocal: Sweller and colleagues state that "split attention occurs very commonly in instructional contexts... the evidence suggests overwhelmingly that it has negative consequences." Labels, annotations, and step-by-step reasoning must be embedded directly on or adjacent to the diagram, not in separate text blocks.

The **redundancy effect** provides a counterintuitive warning: presenting the same information in multiple forms simultaneously hinders learning. In economics textbooks, this means avoiding text that merely describes what a graph already shows. Instead, text should add interpretive value—explaining *why* the curve shifts, what the implications are, or how this connects to previous concepts. Sweller (2016) is emphatic: "Most people assume that providing learners with additional information is at worst, harmless... Redundancy is anything but harmless."

New concepts per section should be **limited to four to six**, aligned with working memory research. Chapter content should be divided into three to four main sections of manageable length, each with built-in formative checks. The simple-to-complex approach (perfect competition before oligopoly, closed economy before open) and the part-whole approach (teach demand separately, then supply, then combine into equilibrium) both effectively manage intrinsic load for high-element-interactivity material typical of economics.

## Graphs must be taught, not just shown

Economics is uniquely suited to dual coding—Paivio's theory that information encoded through both verbal and visual channels creates stronger, more retrievable memories. Supply-demand curves, production possibility frontiers, and circular flow diagrams inherently pair abstract relationships with spatial representations. A 2024 study in *Nature: Humanities and Social Sciences Communications* confirmed that combining visual and verbal information enhances retention in economics specifically.

But graphs do not automatically improve learning. Cohn and colleagues (2001), in controlled experiments at the University of South Carolina, found that students in lectures *with* graphs sometimes scored **significantly lower** than those without—a finding that underscores that *how* graphs are integrated matters more than whether they are present. Ring and Oberrauch (2024), testing 579 students with an 18-item graph competence instrument, identified specific weaknesses: students struggle with comparing quantities along the y-axis, misinterpret what supply and demand curves represent, and find indifference curves particularly challenging. The persistent confusion between **shifts of a curve and movements along a curve** remains the single most common misconception in economics graph interpretation.

Mayer's multimedia learning principles provide the operational playbook. The **segmenting principle** is especially powerful for economics: complex diagrams should be built step by step rather than presented complete. A market equilibrium analysis might use four sequential panels—demand curve alone, supply curve added, equilibrium identified, effect of a shift shown—each with accompanying explanation. This approach aligns with what Chris Makler's EconGraphs platform at Stanford demonstrates digitally, and can be approximated in print with sequential panels.

Authors should maintain a **consistent visual language throughout**: the same color for demand curves (conventionally blue), the same color for supply (red), the same annotation style, the same diagram formatting from chapter one through the final chapter. This consistency builds visual fluency and reduces extraneous load. Crucially, color must never be the sole carrier of meaning—approximately 8% of males are color-blind, so line styles, patterns, and labels must always supplement color. A recommended balance is roughly **60% stylized theoretical diagrams and 40% real-data visualizations**, with clear visual differentiation between the two types. An early "How to Read an Economics Graph" section should pre-train graph-reading skills before the first analytical diagram appears.

## Worked examples, fading, and the power of interleaved practice

The worked example effect is what Sweller calls "the best known and most widely studied of the cognitive load effects." Meta-analysis by Crissman (2006) found an effect size of **0.52** for worked examples. For novice learners, studying step-by-step solutions is significantly more effective than attempting to solve problems independently, because problem-solving via means-ends analysis consumes working memory that could otherwise build mental schemas.

The transition from worked examples to independent practice should follow **backward fading** (Renkl and Atkinson, 2003): start with a fully worked example, then remove the last step for students to complete, then remove progressively more steps, until students solve independently. Backward fading outperforms forward fading because earlier steps have higher element interactivity and benefit most from guidance. For economics, worked examples should cover calculations (elasticity, multiplier, GDP), diagram construction (step-by-step labeling of axes, curves, equilibrium points, welfare areas), chain-of-reasoning arguments (definition → diagram → explanation → application → evaluation), and data interpretation.

A critical nuance: the **expertise reversal effect** means that as learners gain competence, worked examples become counterproductive. This directly supports differentiation—early material should be heavily scaffolded, with scaffolding deliberately reduced as chapters progress.

Exercise progression within a chapter should follow Bloom's taxonomy: define → calculate → explain → analyze → evaluate → create. SOLO taxonomy offers a complementary lens for response quality: unistructural (define opportunity cost) → multistructural (list three demand shifters) → relational (explain how interest rates affect consumption, investment, AD, and employment) → extended abstract (evaluate whether government healthcare intervention is justified and identify conditions where your conclusion might not hold).

Perhaps the strongest finding in learning science applies to textbook exercise design: **interleaved practice**. Rohrer, Dedrick, Hartwig, and Cheung (2020), in a randomized controlled trial, found that interleaving different problem types boosted test scores by approximately **30%** compared to blocked practice. The researchers note there is "no direct evidence that students benefit from solving more than a few problems of the same kind in immediate succession." Yet analysis of six popular math textbooks found only **9.7%** of problems were interleaved. End-of-chapter exercise sets should mix current-chapter content (60%) with problems from two to three previous chapters (25%) and earlier material (15%). Every three to four chapters, a cumulative review section should pull together threads from across the book.

Retrieval practice—Roediger and Karpicke's "testing effect"—deserves deliberate integration. Taking a practice test improves long-term retention more than equivalent time spent restudying, even without feedback. Each chapter should open with a five-question retrieval quiz mixing current and prior topics, include "Check Your Understanding" boxes after each major section, and close with interleaved cumulative exercises. These must be framed as learning tools, not assessment—low-stakes and confidence-building.

## The pitfalls that undermine even well-intentioned textbooks

David Colander's influential critique "Tools, Not Rules" (2016) identifies the core problem: economic principles, when presented without nuance, "undermine students' understanding of economics." Students memorize principles for exams and miss the contextual discussion where nuance exists. Mankiw's response—"The Tradeoff between Nuance and Clarity"—acknowledges the shared goal but argues that nuance introduced too early can muddle the message. The practical resolution is **layered nuance**: introduce clean principles first, then systematically complicate them with qualifications, exceptions, and competing perspectives in subsequent encounters.

**Treating models as reality** rather than as analytical tools is perhaps the most insidious textbook flaw. The Economics in Context Initiative (Boston University/Tufts) explicitly presents models as "thought experiments—human-created analytical tools that may help us gain insight—rather than a set of 'laws' about 'the way the world works.'" Authors should consistently signal the assumptions behind models, their limitations, and the gap between model predictions and observed reality.

Gender bias remains pervasive. Stevenson and Zlotnick's study of seven leading introductory textbooks found that **77% of people mentioned are male**, only 6% of business leaders referenced are female, and women in fictional examples are disproportionately placed in domestic or consumer roles while men handle analysis and decision-making. Authors must deliberately craft examples representing the world students are entering, not the world of the past.

Student misconceptions are deeply resistant to standard instruction. Busom, Lopez-Mayan, and Panadés found that "exposure to an economic principles course and doing well in exams and coursework hardly seems to affect misconceptions." This means textbooks cannot simply present correct information and expect misconceptions to disappear. Evidence-based strategies include predict-observe-explain sequences (present a scenario, ask students to predict, reveal actual outcomes), explicit side-by-side comparison of the misconception and the correct conception, and data-driven exercises where students encounter evidence that contradicts their priors. Davies and Mangan's work on threshold concepts identifies specific sticking points: differentiating price from cost, money from income, real from nominal values, and savings from investment. These deserve explicit, repeated attention.

The problem of **outdated examples** creates a difficult balance. As one Open Textbook Library reviewer noted: "Contemporary examples certainly make a text more relevant, but they also guarantee some impression of being outdated in a few short years." The solution is a layered approach: timeless principles as the structural core, with updatable real-world "wrappers." Teaching students to analyze *types* of events (recessions, trade disputes, inflation episodes) and then providing specific historical instances as illustrations means individual examples can be swapped without restructuring chapters. Digital supplements that update annually can keep case material current while the print core remains stable.

## Differentiation without labeling, and the problem-first approach to engagement

A single textbook can serve students at different levels through what Tomlinson calls differentiation of content, process, product, and learning environment—based on readiness, interest, and learning profile. The most effective strategy is **tiered questions** with neutral framing: Foundation, Standard, and Challenge tiers (common in UK exam board materials) allow all students to access core content while providing stretch opportunities without stigmatization. Extension boxes ("Going Further," "Economist's Notebook," "Research Challenge") should be visibly optional, framed as opportunity rather than remediation. Margin features—glossary definitions, recall boxes, common-misconception alerts, cross-references—serve weaker students without slowing stronger ones. Open-ended tasks naturally accommodate different ability levels: "Analyze the likely effects of a minimum wage increase on your local economy" invites responses at varying levels of sophistication.

The expertise reversal effect provides theoretical justification: novices need scaffolding that becomes counterproductive for advanced learners. This means the *same* chapter can differentiate by including both scaffolded and unscaffolded versions of problems, letting students (or teachers) choose the appropriate entry point.

For engagement, the evidence overwhelmingly supports a **problem-first, theory-second** approach. Velenchik (1995) identified that "undergraduate economics courses tend to treat applications as secondary to the exposition of theory... When students do not understand the purpose of theory, the process of learning becomes more dry and difficult than it needs to be." CORE Econ's first question to students—"What is the most pressing problem that economists should address?"—data from 8,000+ students reveals that climate change, inequality, poverty, and sustainability dominate. Organizing content around these motivating problems, then developing theory as tools to understand them, generates significantly more engagement than theory-first presentation.

Case studies should be focused rather than broad. Student research (Tandfonline, 2023) found students are "willing to engage with complex topics, but did not like overly broad case studies." Focused cases with depth outperform survey-style cases with breadth. Their appearance and presentation matter, as does providing adequate reading time. Velenchik's comparative study found case-method students demonstrated "dramatic improvement in analytical thinking" and performed better on exam questions requiring analysis of real-world situations.

## Blended design is not optional—it is the evidence-based default

Three major meta-analyses (Clinton 2019; Delgado et al. 2018; Kong et al. 2018) confirm a **"screen inferiority effect"** for reading comprehension, particularly for long texts under time pressure. Print remains superior for sustained deep reading, annotation, and spatial orientation within a text. Yet digital uniquely enables interactive graphs, real-time data exploration, adaptive quizzing with instant feedback, simulations, and continuous content updates. The evidence points firmly to a blended model: print for core conceptual exposition, digital for everything that benefits from interactivity and currency.

The most praised platforms illustrate what digital does well. CORE Econ's interactive diagrams let students manipulate curves and observe consequences. Pearson's MyLab Economics integrates real-time FRED data so students explore current macroeconomic indicators. MobLab offers 60+ economics games and simulations. Macmillan's LearningCurve adaptive quizzing adjusts difficulty based on performance. The Dutch *Economie Integraal* exemplifies the philosophy: "Fully in class, full self-study, fully on paper, fully digital, and every possible combination is possible."

For secondary-level economics, the practical recommendation is to design a modular structure where each unit works in print as a self-contained learning experience, while digital components extend rather than duplicate. Krugman's AP textbook demonstrates this with short modules designed as single-lesson units, each with corresponding digital exercises. Static PDFs offer negligible advantage over print; the value of digital lies in interactivity, adaptivity, and real-time data.

## What the best textbooks actually do differently

The most praised economics textbooks at this level share identifiable design patterns. **Krugman's Economics for AP** (Ray and Anderson) earns consistent teacher superlatives—"My students actually read this book!"—through module-based structure (short modules rather than long chapters), AP-aligned practice embedded throughout (seven MCQs and two FRQs per module), margin tips flagging common pitfalls, and concise video explanations. **CORE Econ's *The Economy*** represents the most radical rethink: problem-driven organization, behavioral economics and game theory from the start, interactive digital-first design, and open access. La Trobe University research found students taught using CORE performed better in *all subsequent subjects*. **Blink and Dorton's IB Economics** is valued for its examiner authorship, inquiry-based tasks, and Theory of Knowledge integration. In the Netherlands, **Economie Integraal** offers Bloom's-classified exercises from chapter one and adaptive digital exercises alongside print, while **Pincode** combines current events with modular flexibility.

What unites these is a consistent chapter architecture: narrative hook or real-world problem → prior knowledge activation → clear learning objectives → modular content sections (each 800–1,200 words with built-in formative checks) → worked examples with fading → tiered practice → interleaved review → extension opportunities. This template, applied consistently, creates the predictability that the UBC Open Text Publishing Guide identifies as essential: "The user can get comfortable with the layout, tempo of presentation, and the pattern of figures, illustrations, examples, and exercises."

## Conclusion: the design principles that matter most

Three insights emerge from this research that textbook authors most commonly miss. First, **interleaving is the single highest-leverage change** most economics textbooks fail to implement—mixing problem types across chapters rather than blocking them by topic produces large, reliable learning gains, yet fewer than 10% of textbook problems are currently interleaved. Second, **graphs must be actively taught, not passively displayed**—the evidence that graphs alone do not improve (and can worsen) learning should alarm every economics textbook author who treats diagrams as self-explanatory. Building graphs step by step with integrated annotations, pre-training graph-reading skills, and explicitly addressing the shift-versus-movement confusion are non-negotiable design requirements. Third, **misconceptions require confrontation, not just correct presentation**—standard instruction barely moves misconceptions, so textbooks must include predict-observe-explain sequences, explicit misconception-correction features, and data-driven exercises that create the cognitive conflict necessary for conceptual change.

The overarching principle is that effective textbook design is not about adding more content but about reducing friction between the student and the concept. Every decorative image, every separated legend, every block of same-type practice problems, and every abstract theory introduced without a motivating problem represents friction that evidence says should be eliminated. The best economics textbook is one where every element earns its place by directly supporting schema construction—and nothing else remains.
