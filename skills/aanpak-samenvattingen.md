# Approach: Chapter Summaries for Exam Preparation

Recipe for building chapter-level summary documents that prepare students for exams. Always use together with `econ-word-templates` (for components) and `economic-graph` (for any embedded visuals).

## Purpose
A VWO 4 student (age 15-16) should, after reading the samenvatting, have enough overview to prepare for a test on the entire chapter. The samenvatting does not replace practising exercises, but ensures the student:
- Knows and can explain all terms
- Has all formulas ready and knows when to apply them
- Understands the reasoning chains (cause → effect)
- Recognizes graph types and can interpret them
- Knows which calculations may be asked
- Avoids common mistakes

---

## Structure per samenvatting

### 1. Title page
- Chapter number and name
- Subtitle: "Samenvatting voor toetsvoorbereiding"
- Module label

### 2. Overview: what should you know and be able to do?
A compact table with **all paragraphs** and per paragraph:
- The 2-3 most important learning goals (as active sentences: "Je kunt...")
- Difficulty indicator (★ / ★★ / ★★★)

### 3. Terminology per paragraph
Per paragraph a **terminology table** with:
- Term (bold)
- Definition (clear, compact, in student-friendly language)
- Example or application (concrete, recognizable)

This is the core of the samenvatting. Every term that could be asked on a test must be included — not just the name and definition, but also a concrete example so the student understands what the term means in practice.

### 4. Formulas and calculations
An overview page with **all formulas** from the chapter:
- Formula in a formula-box (Consolas, grey frame)
- Per formula: when do you use it? (1 sentence)
- Worked example using the same numbers as in the presentatie

This section must be complete enough that a student who reads only this page can perform every calculation on the test.

### 5. Reasoning chains
For every important cause-effect relationship in the chapter:
- A **compact reasoning chain** with signal words
- For example: "Import tariff → foreign producer's costs rise → supplies less → price rises → CS falls"

Reasoning questions are the hardest questions on an economics test. By writing out the chains explicitly, students learn the logical steps that are expected.

### 6. Graph overview
Per graph type that appears in the chapter:
- Name of the graph (e.g., "Monopoly graph with MR, MC, ATC")
- **Miniature description**: which lines are in it, what do you read from it?
- Step-by-step plan: how do you use the graph in a calculation?

Many students can handle the formulas but get lost in the graph. By providing a compact overview per graph type, they know exactly what to draw and read off.

### 7. Comparison table (if applicable)
For chapters with multiple variants of the same concept (e.g., market structures):
- A **comparison table** with all variants side by side
- Columns: characteristic | market structure A | market structure B | market structure C
- Rows: number of suppliers, product type, entry barriers, price setting, long-run profit, efficiency

This is the most frequently asked test question type for comparative chapters: "What is the difference between X and Y?"

### 8. Common mistakes
A list of 5-8 common mistakes with:
- The mistake (what students often do)
- The correct approach

This section prevents students from losing points on avoidable errors.

### 9. Exam preparation checklist
A tick-off checklist with "Ik kan..."-statements, grouped by:
- Terms (knowing)
- Calculations (applying)
- Reasoning (applying in context)
- Graphs (drawing/interpreting)

## Design principles

### Completeness over brevity
The samenvatting must be **complete**. Everything that could be asked on a test must be in it. Better 8 pages with everything included than 3 pages where the student still needs the textbook afterwards.

### Concrete examples, not abstract definitions
Every term gets a concrete example. Not: "Comparative advantage = producing relatively cheaper." But: "Comparative advantage = NL can produce tulips relatively cheaper than cars → NL specializes in tulips."

### Formulas with context
Not just the formula, but also: when do you use it? And a worked example. The student should immediately know which formula applies when seeing a test question.

### Visually structured
- Terminology tables with alternating row colors
- Formula-boxes with accent stripes
- Tip-boxes for common mistakes
- Reasoning chains as compact chains
- Checklist with tick-off boxes

### Language and level
- Clear Dutch, no academic jargon
- Active sentences ("Je berekent..." not "Er wordt berekend...")
- Examples from the world of 15-16-year-olds
- Signal words explicitly named ("doordat", "waardoor", "hierdoor")

---

## Sources per samenvatting
Content is compiled from:
1. **Basisopgaven antwoorden** — the skills and learning goals
2. **Presentaties** — the core concepts and graphs
3. **Uitleg vaardigheden** — the formulas and step-by-step plans
4. **Uitleg voorkennis** — the required prior knowledge

---

## Size
- Chapter with 3 paragraphs: ~6-8 pages
- Chapter with 5-7 paragraphs: ~10-14 pages
- "Toepassen" paragraphs are not summarized separately but incorporated into the comparison table and reasoning chains
